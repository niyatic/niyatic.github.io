We fine-tuned open models on financial documents. The honest result is "a narrow edge," not "domination" — and that's the more useful finding.

On SAVIOR-Bench v1 — a 509-document, expert-annotated financial-document benchmark — three rows are open-source models LoRA fine-tuned by Hyperbots on in-domain data (SAVIOR-Train, Hyperbots IP). Every other system in the panel is zero-shot in its vendor-native setting.

That distinction is not a footnote, it is the frame. Comparing a model trained on this exact document distribution against models that have never seen it is not apples-to-apples — the fine-tune carries a structural advantage by construction. So the honest question isn't "did it win," it's "by how much, and where did it not win at all."

The answer, with every comparison disclosed:

• Parse-intent F1: fine-tuned 0.8328 vs best well-documented zero-shot 0.8052 — a +0.0276 edge.
• OCR recall: fine-tuned 0.9294 vs best zero-shot 0.9233 — a +0.0061 edge.
• OCR precision: a zero-shot vision LLM wins, by 0.0165.
• Layout fidelity (PaIRS): a zero-shot OCR-plus-rules stack wins, by 0.17.

We kept the two losing rows in the headline chart on purpose. Cropping them would have misrepresented the result. The fine-tune advantage is real but recall- and extraction-specific — it is not a precision or layout story.

The practical lesson for anyone choosing a document-AI approach: a domain fine-tune buys you consistent in-domain recall and field extraction at small (3B/7B) model sizes — a meaningful operating point — not a blowout on every metric. And distrust any "fine-tuned model dominates" claim that won't show you the metrics where it loses.

The Narrow, Honest Edge

Deep technical blog · ~1,950 words · angle: fine-tuned vs zero-shot results.

1. The claim everyone wants to make

If you fine-tune an open model on your own domain data and then benchmark it against the zero-shot field, there is an enormous temptation to write one sentence: "our fine-tuned model dominates." It is punchy, it converts, and on SAVIOR-Bench v1 it would be wrong — or at least so incomplete as to be misleading.

SAVIOR-Bench v1 is a 509-document, expert-annotated financial-document benchmark spanning 21 OCR systems and 23 parse-intent systems. Three rows in that panel are open-source models LoRA fine-tuned by Hyperbots on SAVIOR-Train, an in-domain financial-document training set that is Hyperbots IP: hyperapi-parse-intent, Qwen2.5-VL-7B-F, and Qwen2.5-VL-3B-F. Every other row is zero-shot in its vendor-native setting. This post is about what those three fine-tunes actually bought — disclosed honestly, including the metrics where they lost.

2. Why the comparison is not apples-to-apples — and why we say so first

Before any number, the disclosure that governs all of them. A LoRA fine-tune on SAVIOR-Train has, by construction, seen the distribution that SAVIOR-Bench evaluates. The zero-shot systems have not. A fine-tuned row beating a zero-shot row is therefore not a like-for-like contest — it is a model trained on this document distribution measured against models that were not. The structural advantage sits with the fine-tune before a single token is decoded.

This is not a reason to hide the comparison; it is a reason to label it. In the SAVIOR-Bench tables every fine-tuned row carries an inline, non-removable [FT] tag, and the report's modesty anchor is deliberately a general-purpose vision LLM in zero-shot — the strongest well-documented zero-shot parse-intent system — so the headline gap is measured against a credible, transparent baseline rather than a weak one. With that frame fixed, the numbers are honest to read.

3. Parse-intent: a real, modest lead

Parse-intent is the structured task — F1 over extracted intent fields (higher is better), WER over the transcription (lower is better). The top of the 23-system table:

RankModelF1 ↑WER ↓Setting
1hyperapi-parse-intent [FT]0.83280.2589fine-tuned
2DocIntentOCR-3B (under-documented)0.81020.5622zero-shot
3general-purpose vision LLM (well-doc anchor)0.80520.4232zero-shot

The fine-tuned model leads. Against the well-documented zero-shot anchor the F1 edge is +0.0276; against the under-documented rank-2 system it is +0.0226. That is a genuine lead and worth having — but it is one to three points of F1, not a different league. Where the fine-tune separates more cleanly is WER: 0.2589 against the anchor's 0.4232. The fine-tune transcribes the in-domain text more faithfully, and that lower error rate is what feeds a cleaner downstream field extraction. The honest framing: a meaningful, recall-and-fidelity-shaped edge, not a landslide.

4. OCR recall: six thousandths

On the 509-document OCR task, recall is the headline a fine-tune is supposed to win — and it does, barely:

ModelRecall ↑Precision ↑Setting
Qwen2.5-VL-7B-F [FT]0.92940.9157fine-tuned
legacy IDP system (best zero-shot recall)0.92330.8902zero-shot
Qwen2.5-VL-3B-F [FT]0.91670.9101fine-tuned

The fine-tuned 7B model tops recall at 0.9294. The best zero-shot system — a mature legacy IDP cloud pipeline — sits at 0.9233. The gap is +0.0061. Six thousandths. A well-engineered zero-shot OCR pipeline is nearly even with an in-domain fine-tune on recall. The 3B fine-tune, notably, also lands above the 0.91 line — which is the real operational story (more on that in section 6).

5. Where zero-shot wins — and why we keep those rows

Here is the part a hype post deletes. The SAVIOR-Bench headline chart has four metric groups, and fine-tuning leads on two of them. On the other two it loses:

Metric groupBest FTBest ZSDeltaWinner
OCR recall ↑0.92940.9233+0.0061FT, narrowly
Parse-intent F1 ↑0.83280.8052+0.0276FT
OCR precision ↑0.91570.9322−0.0165ZS
Layout / PaIRS ↑0.70660.8766−0.1700ZS

On precision, a zero-shot general-purpose vision LLM wins (0.9322 vs 0.9157). On layout fidelity, measured by PaIRS, a zero-shot OCR-plus-rules stack wins by a wide 0.17 — none of the three fine-tunes leads layout at all; the best FT PaIRS is 0.7068. One caveat strengthens the honesty rather than rescuing the result: PaIRS is directly comparable only among prompt-controlled (PC) models, and for non-prompt-controlled systems it is a format-compatibility proxy. But even read conservatively, fine-tuning did not buy a layout win.

The report retains both ZS-leading rows in the headline chart "by design — cropping them would misrepresent the result." That single sentence is the whole ethic of this blog. A four-row chart where you only show the two rows you win is not a chart, it is an advertisement.

6. So what does fine-tuning actually buy?

Read the four deltas together and a precise — and genuinely favorable — story emerges. Fine-tuning on in-domain financial data does not produce a model that dominates every axis of document understanding. It produces a model with the best in-domain recall and the best structured parse-intent F1 and WER in the panel, and it does so at small model sizes: the recall leader is a 7B, and the 3B fine-tune still clears 0.91 recall. That is the operating point that matters. A 3B model that matches or beats a heavyweight zero-shot field on the two metrics that drive in-domain extraction is a cost and latency win, not just an accuracy footnote — and it is exactly the kind of edge a domain fine-tune should produce.

What it does not buy is a precision or layout crown. If your workload is dominated by faithful layout reconstruction, a strong zero-shot OCR-plus-rules stack remains competitive or better. Fine-tuning is a targeted instrument: it moves recall and extraction fidelity on the distribution you trained on. Used for that, the three SAVIOR-Bench fine-tunes are the right tool and the panel shows it. Sold as a universal win, the same numbers would be a misrepresentation.

7. How to read any fine-tune-vs-zero-shot claim

The transferable discipline: never accept a fine-tune-vs-zero-shot comparison without the disclosure that it is not apples-to-apples, demand the metrics where the fine-tune lost, prefer a modesty anchor that is a strong, well-documented baseline rather than a weak strawman, and check the model sizes — a narrow edge at 3B is a different and better result than the same edge at 70B. SAVIOR-Bench v1 publishes all four. The fine-tunes come out ahead where a domain fine-tune should, by a margin the report never inflates — and that combination, the favorable result and the honest losing rows, is what makes the favorable result believable.

The Narrow, Honest Edge: Quantifying What In-Domain Fine-Tuning Buys on a Financial-Document Benchmark

Hyperbots Research · SAVIOR-Bench Working Group · snapshot 2026-05-20
Abstract. We characterize the performance of three open-source models LoRA fine-tuned on in-domain financial-document data against a zero-shot panel of 21 OCR and 23 parse-intent systems on SAVIOR-Bench v1. The fine-tuned models lead on OCR recall (+0.0061) and parse-intent F1 (+0.0276 vs the best well-documented zero-shot anchor) but trail on OCR precision (-0.0165) and layout fidelity (PaIRS, -0.1700). We disclose all fine-tuned-vs-zero-shot comparisons as not apples-to-apples — a fine-tune has seen the evaluated distribution — and retain the zero-shot-leading rows in the headline chart. The edge is real, narrow, and metric-specific: in-domain recall and structured extraction, at 3B/7B model sizes.

1. Introduction

In-domain fine-tuning is often marketed as categorically superior to zero-shot inference. We test that on SAVIOR-Bench v1 with full disclosure that fine-tuned and zero-shot settings are not directly comparable.

2. Method

Three rows are open-source models LoRA fine-tuned by Hyperbots on SAVIOR-Train (in-domain, Hyperbots IP): hyperapi-parse-intent, Qwen2.5-VL-7B-F, Qwen2.5-VL-3B-F. All other rows are zero-shot vendor-native. Tasks: parse-intent (F1, WER) and OCR (word-level recall/precision, PaIRS layout). One frozen prompt per family; decoding pinned (temp 0.1, top-p 0.9, seed 20260516). The parse-intent modesty anchor is the best well-documented zero-shot system.

3. Results

MetricBest FTBest ZSΔ
OCR recall0.92940.9233+0.0061
Parse-intent F10.83280.8052+0.0276
OCR precision0.91570.9322-0.0165
Layout PaIRS0.70660.8766-0.1700

Fine-tuning leads recall and parse-intent F1/WER; zero-shot leads precision and layout. PaIRS is directly comparable only among prompt-controlled models.

4. Limitations

Fine-tuned-vs-zero-shot is not apples-to-apples: the fine-tunes were trained on the evaluated distribution. No confidence intervals (ci95 = null). No per-class slices. Parse-intent n not reported. Cells attested 2026-05-18, not factory-reproduced. Benchmark figures are model performance, not product accuracy.

5. Conclusion

In-domain fine-tuning buys a real but narrow, metric-specific edge — in-domain recall and structured extraction at small model sizes — not universal dominance. Honest reporting retains the metrics where zero-shot wins.
