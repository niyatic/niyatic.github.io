Hyperbots fine-tuned models vs the document-AI category

**DRAFT — internal only. External publication is human-gated. This study refers

to category approaches by label only ("legacy IDP", "OCR-plus-rules",

"general-purpose vision LLM", "zero-shot VLM") and names no vendor. The named,

governed comparison lives in the SAVIOR-Bench benchmark artifact, linked below

and not restated here.**

The honest headline finding

If you came expecting "our fine-tune crushes the field," that is not what the

data says — and saying so would not survive contact with a careful reader.

**The honest, non-obvious finding: the fine-tune edge is real, but narrow — and

narrowly scoped.** On SAVIOR-Bench, Hyperbots' fine-tuned models lead on exactly

two metrics, by small margins, and *lose* on two others to strong zero-shot

systems. Specifically:

  • On parse-intent F1, the fine-tuned hyperapi-parse-intent leads the best

*well-documented* zero-shot system by +0.0276 F1 (0.8328 vs 0.8052).

  • On OCR recall, the fine-tuned Qwen2.5-VL-7B-F leads the best zero-shot

system by only +0.0061 recall (0.9294 vs 0.9233).

  • On OCR precision, a zero-shot general-purpose vision LLM leads both

fine-tuned rows (0.9322 vs 0.9157).

  • On layout fidelity (PaIRS), zero-shot systems lead by a wide margin

(0.8766 vs 0.7066).

That is the real shape of the result. The interesting part is not the size of

the win — it is *why a narrow win is still the right approach*. Fine-tuning on

financial-document data buys a consistent recall/F1 edge and a large word-

error-rate reduction (parse-intent WER 0.2589 vs the zero-shot anchor's 0.4232),

and it does so on small open backbones (3B / 7B parameters) at roughly

$0.19 per 1,000 successful calls. The category's frontier zero-shot systems

are far larger and far more expensive per call. So the question a buyer should

ask is not "who is +0.05 ahead on a leaderboard" — it is "which approach gives a

predictable, ownable, cheap-to-serve transcription path for financial documents."

On that question the fine-tune story holds up; on a raw-magnitude story it does

not, and we will not tell the raw-magnitude story.

Methodology — read this before any number

Every number below is grounded in SAVIOR-Bench v1, a 509-document,

expert-annotated financial-document benchmark spanning five corpora (UCSF

Industry Documents, in-house synthetic, misc. public financial, Inv3D, FUNSD),

with an inter-annotator agreement of 0.761 (fuzzy string similarity over 187

double-annotated pairs).

  • Two task families, identical inputs. (a) OCR transcription + layout

fidelity, scored by word-level multiset recall and precision plus

PaIRS (a pairwise spatial-relationship layout score); (b) parse-intent

structured extraction, scored by F1 (↑) and word error rate / WER (↓).

  • Frozen decoding. Temperature 0.1, top-p 0.9, max_tokens 2048, seed

20260516; one frozen prompt template per task family.

  • Panel. ~21 OCR systems and 23 parse-intent systems spanning the full

category — cloud document-AI APIs, frontier general-purpose vision LLMs, and

open-source OCR/VLM stacks. Three rows are Hyperbots fine-tunes on

SAVIOR-Train (LoRA, rank 32 / α 64, on Qwen2.5-VL-3B/7B open backbones);

every other row is zero-shot in its native settings.

  • Provenance. Every cell is in-house-run, user-attested 2026-05-18

(source: benchmark.xlsx + the in-repo verbatim extract of the

Hyperbots-provided source PDF). The numbers are not factory-harness-

reproduced. Confidence intervals are not reported (ci95=null); no

interval is fabricated. n=509 for the OCR table; n is not reported in the

attested source for the parse-intent table, and we do not infer one.

These figures are model OCR/parse performance — not product accuracy. Any

Hyperbots product-accuracy figure would be cited only "per apis.hyperbots.com";

this study makes no product-accuracy claim.

Not apples-to-apples — the fine-tune-vs-zero-shot disclosure

This is the single most important caveat in the study, and it is stated here,

inline, before the leaderboard tables — exactly as the benchmark paper states it

in its abstract.

Three rows are fine-tuned [FT]; every other row is zero-shot. The two
leading OCR rows in our favour (Qwen2.5-VL-7B-F, Qwen2.5-VL-3B-F) and the
leading parse-intent system (hyperapi-parse-intent) are **fine-tuned by
Hyperbots on SAVIOR-Train** (LoRA on open backbones — Hyperbots IP). Every
other panel row is zero-shot in the vendor's native settings. A fine-tuned
model compared against a zero-shot model is **not an apples-to-apples
comparison** — the fine-tuned model has seen task-matched financial-document
training data and the zero-shot models have not. We mark fine-tuned rows
[FT] everywhere they appear. Every delta below is direction-only — with
ci95=null, no delta is an interval-significant claim, and the hedging words
("narrow", "modest", "marginally") are the honest qualifier.

How Hyperbots fine-tunes compare to each category approach

  • vs legacy IDP. Legacy IDP is template/schema-bound and brittle on

unseen layouts; SAVIOR-Bench's value is precisely that it scores six document

classes and five corpora on identical inputs. A fine-tuned open model adapts

to the corpus distribution rather than to a fixed schema — but this study has

no head-to-head legacy-IDP cell, so this is a category-shape observation, not

a measured claim.

  • vs OCR-plus-rules. SAVIOR-Bench's word-level (not char-level) scoring

exists because downstream extraction needs whole tokens — Totl ≠ Total.

OCR-plus-rules stacks tend to score moderate field-F1 while transcription WER

explodes (the benchmark shows zero-shot systems with F1 ~0.73 at WER ~3.0).

The fine-tuned parse-intent system's WER of 0.2589 is its strongest result.

  • vs general-purpose vision LLM. A frontier general-purpose vision LLM is

the *best zero-shot* system on OCR precision (0.9322) and a strong parse-intent

baseline (0.8052 F1). It is genuinely competitive — and far larger and more

expensive to serve than a 3B/7B fine-tune. The fine-tune trades a small

precision deficit for recall, WER, ownership, and cost.

  • vs zero-shot VLM. Across the panel, zero-shot VLMs span the full range

(parse-intent F1 0.34–0.81). The fine-tune's edge over the *best* zero-shot

VLM is narrow; its edge over the *median* is large. The honest claim is

"fine-tuning lifts a small open backbone to the top of the panel," not

"fine-tuning beats every approach by a wide margin."

Operational profile (model performance, not a price quote)

From the attested source, on the SAVIOR-Train fine-tuned inference path:

  • Latency: p50 2,548 ms · p99 3,319 ms.
  • Failure rate: 3% (KV-cache-bound; a 2-GPU path is the noted mitigation).
  • Cost: ~$0.000186 per attempted call, ~$0.000191 per successful call —

≈ $0.19 per 1,000 successful calls on an A100 40 GB at ~$2/hr.

These are infrastructure-derived figures for the model, not a Hyperbots product

price and not a product-accuracy claim.

Honesty note — the W6 0/23 factory-reproduction audit

We will not present these numbers as more verified than they are.

On 2026-05-21, the pinned factory harness was run against all 23 rows of the

parse-intent table under the W6 workstream. The outcome: **0/23 cells

re-derived green; 23/23 red on structural impossibility; 0 confidence intervals

added; 0 attested rows retracted; 0 silent metric flips.**

The three structural reasons — all recorded in the audit log — are: (i) the SEED

harness, by design, is offline / stdlib-only / no-API-key and has no adapters

for the 23 hosted-API vendor models; (ii) the in-house parse-intent corpus is

not in the benchmark repository, so its loader is not registered; (iii) the

attested source carries only aggregate F1/WER per model — no per-document

score vector exists, so a bootstrap confidence interval cannot be derived without

fabricating per-document scores, which the team refused to do.

Why this does not retract the numbers, and what it does mean. The factory

harness did not produce a *competing* measurement that disagrees with the

attested one — it produced *no* measurement. Absence of an independent

re-derivation is not evidence of error. The attested numbers stand at their

documented provenance tier: *in-house-run, user-attested 2026-05-18; not

factory-harness-reproduced; ci95=null.* What W6 contributes is evidence of due

diligence — the harness was actually run, the blockers were named, nothing was

silently flipped. A reader should treat every number in this study as

attested, not reproduced, and weight it accordingly. That is the honest tier,

and we state it rather than imply more.

What this means for a document-AI buyer

The defensible takeaway is narrow and true:

1. Fine-tuning a small open backbone on financial-document data does move it

to the top of a 23-system panel on parse-intent F1 and to the top of a

21-system panel on OCR recall.

2. The margin over the *best* zero-shot system is small (+0.0061 recall,

+0.0276 F1) — this is not a blowout, and anyone who tells you it is has

cropped the chart.

3. Fine-tuning does not buy precision or layout fidelity; strong zero-shot

systems lead there.

4. The real advantage is the *package*: top-of-panel recall/F1, a large WER

reduction, on small open backbones (Hyperbots IP), at ~$0.19 / 1,000 calls.

5. All of this is attested, not factory-reproduced — read it at that tier.

That is the honest case for the fine-tune approach. It is a narrower case than a

hype post would make, and it is the one that survives a careful reader.
