$0.19 per 1,000 Calls

Deep technical blog · ~1,850 words · angle: cost / latency / throughput economics.

1. The benchmark nobody runs

Accuracy leaderboards are a saturated genre. Every document-AI vendor has a recall number, an F1 number, a layout score. What almost nobody publishes — and what actually decides whether a system survives a production rollout — is the operational profile: how long the slow requests take, how often calls fail and why, and how much a correct answer costs once you account for the ones that didn't work.

SAVIOR-Bench v1, a 509-document expert-annotated financial-document benchmark, reports these alongside the accuracy tables. This post is about the four operational numbers, what they mean, and — just as importantly — what the report deliberately refuses to claim about them.

2. What was measured — and what path it describes

Disclosure before numbers. The operational figures describe one specific path: the SAVIOR-Train fine-tuned inference path — an open-source model LoRA fine-tuned by Hyperbots on in-domain financial-document data (Hyperbots IP) — served on a single NVIDIA A100 40GB at an assumed rate of roughly $2/hr. These are not generalized claims about every model in the panel, and they are not the operational profile of the zero-shot vendor systems. They are this path, on this hardware, at this operating point. Read them that way.

3. Latency: the tail is the product

The median request completes in 2,548 ms. That is a usable interactive latency for a document-extraction call. But the median is not where production pain lives — the tail is. The 99th-percentile latency is 3,319 ms.

The ratio matters more than either number alone. p99 / p50 here is about 1.30 — the slowest one-in-a-hundred request takes only ~30% longer than the typical one. For document AI, that is a notably tight tail. Many systems, especially those that fan out to OCR plus a rules layer plus a re-ranking step, exhibit p99/p50 ratios of 3x, 5x, or worse, because each stage adds its own tail and the tails compound. A path whose p99 is within a third of its median has a predictable worst case — and a predictable worst case is what lets you set an SLA you can actually keep.

What the report explicitly does not provide: p90, p999, or a full latency histogram. The source-of-record gives exactly three latency points, so exactly three are reported. No intermediate percentile is interpolated and no tail beyond p99 is asserted. If you need p999 for a stringent SLA, that is a measurement still to be taken — and the report says so rather than inventing it.

4. Failure rate: 3%, with a named cause

The path has a 3% failure rate. A bare failure-rate number is nearly useless — you cannot engineer against "3% of things break." What makes this one actionable is that the report names the mechanism: the failures are KV-cache bound. Under concurrency, the key/value attention cache on a single 40GB A100 is the binding resource; when it saturates, requests fail rather than silently degrade.

Because the cause is named, the mitigation is concrete: the report notes a 2-GPU serving path. More KV-cache headroom across two devices relieves the exact bottleneck identified. This is the difference between a failure rate as a complaint and a failure rate as an engineering ticket. A vendor who can tell you why calls fail can tell you what it costs to make them stop failing.

5. Cost per call vs cost per correct — and why the gap is the story

Now the economics. Two figures, infrastructure-derived from the A100 hourly rate and the measured throughput:

MetricValueMeaning
Cost per attempted call$0.000186every call, including the ones that failed
Cost per successful call$0.000191cost amortized only over calls that worked

Cost-per-attempt bills you for compute spent on failures. Cost-per-correct re-amortizes that same spend over only the successful calls — it is the true unit cost of a usable answer. The honest version of an economics claim always reports the second number, because the second number is the one that survives contact with a real failure rate.

Here is the subtle, favorable detail: the two numbers are almost identical — $0.000186 versus $0.000191, a gap of roughly 3%. That gap is small because the failure rate is small. If the path failed 30% of the time, cost-per-correct would balloon far above cost-per-attempt and the headline cost would be a fiction. A near-flat curve between attempted and successful cost is the signature of a path whose failures are rare enough that they barely move the unit economics. The 3% failure rate and the tight cost gap are the same fact told twice.

In round terms: roughly $0.19 per 1,000 successful calls. One important boundary — this is an infrastructure-derived figure, computed from the A100 hourly rate and throughput. It is not a price quote, not a list price, and not a product-pricing claim. It is what the compute costs at this operating point. Any actual product pricing would be cited only per apis.hyperbots.com.

6. Why a small model makes the economics work

The operating point is not an accident of generous hardware — it is a consequence of model size. The fine-tuned path runs models in the 3B–7B range. On SAVIOR-Bench v1 the 3B fine-tune still clears 0.91 OCR recall and the 7B fine-tune tops the recall table at 0.9294. A small model that holds in-domain accuracy is what makes a single A100 sufficient, the KV-cache pressure manageable, and the per-call cost land in fractions of a cent. The accuracy result and the economics result are linked: fine-tuning a small open model on in-domain data is what produces both a competitive recall number and a sub-$0.0002 cost-per-correct. Neither figure is impressive alone; together they describe a path that is cheap because it is small and viable because the fine-tune kept it accurate.

7. The operational checklist for buyers

The transferable practice, if you are evaluating any document-AI system: do not accept a median latency without the p99 — the tail is the SLA. Do not accept a failure rate without its named cause and a mitigation — an unexplained failure rate cannot be engineered against. Do not accept cost-per-call when cost-per-correct exists — the two diverge exactly when it matters most. And treat any cost figure as infrastructure-derived unless it is explicitly a quoted price.

SAVIOR-Bench v1 reports p50 and p99, the failure rate and its cause, and both cost figures — and it stops exactly where the source data stops, declining to invent p999 or a histogram. That combination, the favorable operating point reported with the boundaries of what was measured, is what makes the favorable operating point worth believing.

$0.19 per 1,000 Calls: Operational Economics of a Fine-Tuned Document-AI Inference Path

Hyperbots Research · SAVIOR-Bench Working Group · snapshot 2026-05-20
Abstract. Accuracy benchmarks rarely report the operational properties that govern production viability. We present the latency, failure-rate, and cost profile of the SAVIOR-Train fine-tuned inference path on SAVIOR-Bench v1 — an open model LoRA fine-tuned on in-domain financial-document data, served on a single A100 40GB. Latency is p50 2,548 ms and p99 3,319 ms (p99/p50 ≈ 1.30, a tight tail); the failure rate is 3%, KV-cache bound, with a 2-GPU mitigation noted. Cost per attempted call is $0.000186 and cost per successful call $0.000191 (≈ $0.19 per 1,000 successful calls); the small gap reflects the low failure rate. We report only the three latency points the source provides and assert no p90/p999/histogram. Cost figures are infrastructure-derived, not price quotes.

1. Introduction

Document-AI evaluation over-indexes on accuracy and under-reports operational behavior. We characterize latency tails, failure rate with cause, and cost-per-correct for one disclosed inference path.

2. Method

Path: an open-source model LoRA fine-tuned by Hyperbots on in-domain SAVIOR-Train data (Hyperbots IP), served on a single A100 40GB at ~$2/hr. Cost figures are infrastructure-derived from hourly rate and measured throughput. Latency reported at the three points the source-of-record provides (p50, p99) plus the failure rate.
