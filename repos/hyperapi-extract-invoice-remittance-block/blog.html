The invoice field fraud targets: extract the remittance block cleanly

Cross-vendor framing: prebuilt invoice/extract models from large vendors typically collapse on the long tail (continuation pages, layout drift, multi-language) — the deterministic pass/fail approach below is rendered against that backdrop, with measured rates published at apis.hyperbots.com.

Audience: AP / AR / GL operations leaders (sub-ledger owners) · 5 min read

Every accounts-payable pipeline reads vendor and totals off an invoice. The field that actually moves money — the remittance block — is the one most often skipped, and the one fraud rewrites. hyperapi-extract-invoice-remittance-block turns that block into one function call: remit-to party and address, bank name, account/IBAN, routing/SWIFT, and the payment reference, as a typed, validated object with a confidence score on every field.

The hard part isn't OCR

It's that the bank line is a 7-point footer on page 2 on one template and a boxed panel on the next, and an IBAN reads DE44 5001 ... here and de4450010517... there. The library strips whitespace and upper-cases IBAN/BIC so two reads of the same account compare equal, runs a has_bank_routing() coverage check, and surfaces the service's per-field confidence verbatim. It does not checksum the IBAN — that is a validation task, named honestly.

No queue, just a flag

A weak first pass — or a remit-to name with no routable identifier — is automatically re-extracted on a higher-resolution profile; the pass that carries a routable identifier wins. Documents that stay weak come back trustworthy=false with the weak fields named, for your automation to route by rule.

Five lines

``python from hyperapi_extract_invoice_remittance_block import extract_file r = extract_file("invoice.pdf") print(r.block.iban or r.block.account_number, r.trustworthy) ``

Accuracy is published at apis.hyperbots.com. The extraction model is an open-source document model fine-tuned by Hyperbots (Hyperbots IP); this repo is the typed client around it.
