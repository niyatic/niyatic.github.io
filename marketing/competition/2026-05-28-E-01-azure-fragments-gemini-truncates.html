Most document-AI accuracy claims are made on the easy 80% of documents. The

interesting question lives in the other 20% — the long invoices, the ones with

100+ line items, the ones a controller actually loses sleep over. We took the

ten longest invoices in dataset_95 (49–323 line items) and ran them

through the two leading cloud prebuilt extractors. Both broke. They broke

*differently*. And the boundary that breaks them is the same boundary that, today,

breaks ours.

The two failures

Azure DI prebuilt-invoice fragments. On the long-invoice subset (n=10,

factory-reproduced 2026-05-25):

  • line-count preservation: 0.6396 mean — exact match on only 1 of 10 docs
  • line-item-set F1: 0.3439 mean
  • summary total: 9 of 9 matched — it reads the *total* fine, it just can't keep the *line items* straight

The cleanest illustration is 51.json — a 323-line-item invoice. Azure DI

returned 332 rows: close on the *count* (preservation 0.9729) but F1 0.0094 —

1 true positive, 109 false positives, 101 false negatives. It produced a

plausible-looking table that was almost entirely wrong at the row level. On

42.json (115 line items) it returned 0 rows. On 3.json (88 gold rows) it

fragmented the table into 2,150 rows (preservation 0.041).

Gemini 2.5 truncates. Full-n on dataset_95 (factory-reproduced):

Two things stand out. First, the premium model loses — Flash (0.7132) beats

Pro (0.6195) on this corpus, at one-third the cost. Second, the error docs are

*the longest invoices* — the same 42.json and 51.json Azure DI failed on.

Gemini hits a hard JSON-truncation cliff at max_output_tokens=32768 on invoices

with 100+ line items: the model simply stops emitting before the table is done.

The failure *modes* differ — Gemini stops early, Azure DI sprays rows — but the

dataset boundary is identical: invoices past ~100 line items break both

leading prebuilt extractors. This isn't a vendor-specific bug. It's a structural

property of treating a 300-row financial table as one end-to-end generation.

The honest part: ours doesn't win this corpus either

Here is the line most vendor blogs would delete. On dataset_95, the current

Hyperbots IDP chain lands at 0.3573 micro-F1 — below Gemini Flash's 0.7132

(factory-reproduced; shown in the parse-deep benchmark framework, not hidden).

We publish that because the credibility of everything else we say depends on it.

So this post is not "we win." It's "here is the measured frontier, and here is

why the *architecture* — not the model — is the answer to it."

Why API-native, primitive-resolved is the structural fix

The reason a 300-row invoice breaks an end-to-end extractor is that *extraction*

is being asked to do *splitting*, *layout reasoning*, and *table-structure

recovery* all in one shot, inside one token budget. Decompose it and the cliff

moves:

  • split as a first-class primitive: a 323-row invoice is chunked at

page/section boundaries before extraction, so no single call has to emit 332

rows under a 32k-token ceiling.

  • parse → validate as separate, measurable steps: line-count

preservation becomes an *auditable* boundary, not a silent truncation.

  • Open-source backbones fine-tuned by Hyperbots (Hyperbots IP), served per

primitive, so each step is measured and improvable independently — which is

exactly how 0.3573 becomes a number that moves.

That's the bet: not a better single model, but a pipeline where the long-invoice

boundary is a *handled case* instead of a *cliff*.

What a buyer should take from this

1. Ask any vendor for their numbers on invoices past 100 line items, not the

blended average. The cliff lives there.

2. Distinguish summary-total accuracy (Azure DI nails it, 9/9) from

line-item accuracy (0.3439 F1). A tool can be right about the total and

useless for the GL.

3. The premium model is not automatically the right one — Flash beat Pro here at

1/3 the cost. Measure, don't assume.
