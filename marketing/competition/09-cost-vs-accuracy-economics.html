Cost vs accuracy — the operational economics of document-AI at scale

At 50 documents a month, document-AI cost is a rounding error. At 5 million a

month, the pricing model *is* the decision. And cost cannot be evaluated apart

from accuracy — a cheap API that fails 8% of documents is not cheap once you

price the failures. This post is the operational-economics framework.

The three cost views — show them together

The cost-normalization convention requires three figures, always shown together.

A single one in isolation is misleading.

1. Per-page

The vendor-documented unit price for a page-equivalent input. For token-priced

LLM-vision approaches, this is *computed* from a documented tokens-per-page

assumption, calibrated from the actual test corpus — inputs and outputs counted

separately if priced separately. Per-page is what vendor pricing pages quote; it

is the *least* decision-useful view on its own.

2. Per-call

Total observed spend ÷ number of API calls. This makes a page-priced approach and

a token-priced approach comparable apples-to-apples — without it you cannot

compare a hyperscaler doc-AI API to a frontier LLM-vision model at all.

3. Per-correct-extraction

Total observed spend ÷ count of extractions that passed the scoring rubric.

This is the most honest figure, because it penalizes approaches that need retries

or produce errors. A 2-cent-per-page API with a 90% success rate has a

*per-correct* cost of ~2.2 cents; a 3-cent API at 99% has a per-correct cost of

~3.03 cents — and the gap narrows or inverts once you add the cost of handling

the failures.

A critical fairness rule: **no manual-review augmentation is assumed for any

approach, including HyperAPI.** The per-correct figure is computed purely from

scored automated output. A vendor that quotes accuracy "after review" is mixing a

human-process cost into a model number.

The cost of a wrong extraction

Per-correct cost only captures the *direct* waste of a failed call. The real

operational cost of an error is larger and depends on the error mode:

  • A refusal_no_output is the cheapest failure — the document is simply routed

to manual review. Cost ≈ a human's handling time.

  • A numeric_format_error or layout_misread that is *caught* downstream

(e.g. by an arithmetic-reconciliation check) costs a review cycle.

  • A numeric_format_error that is *not caught* — a wrong total posted to the

ledger — is the expensive one. The cost is the downstream correction, the

reconciliation effort, and the audit exposure.

This is why accuracy and cost are one decision, not two. A model's *error-mode

mix* changes the true cost: a model that fails by refusing is cheaper to operate

than one that fails by confidently posting a wrong number, even at the same

headline accuracy.

Token-priced approaches: the variance problem

For LLM-vision approaches, token cost is reported *separately*, because the same

prompt on a different document size produces wildly different unit economics. A

dense 3-page statement can cost several times what a sparse 1-page invoice costs

on the identical pipeline. Both list price and the bulk tier a typical enterprise

qualifies for should be shown — the gap is large and material at volume. A

per-page number that assumes an "average" document hides this variance.

Where the Hyperbots numbers stand

From the comparative study, on the fine-tuned inference path:

  • ~$0.000191 per successful call — about **$0.19 per 1,000 successful

calls** — derived from an A100 40 GB at ~$2/hr.

  • Latency p50 ~2,548 ms, p99 ~3,319 ms; a 3% failure rate (KV-cache-bound, with

a 2-GPU path as the noted mitigation).

Three honesty flags on these numbers:

1. This is an infrastructure-derived model-serving cost, not a Hyperbots

product price. Any HyperAPI product price would be cited only "per

apis.hyperbots.com." Do not read $0.19/1k as a quote.

2. It is a per-successful-call figure on the model path — adjacent to, but

not identical to, the per-correct-extraction metric, which requires the

scoring rubric to be applied to product output.

3. The economic advantage the comparative study claims is the *package* — small

open backbones (3B/7B) are cheap to serve relative to frontier-scale

zero-shot systems — not a measured cost win over any named competitor.

The buyer's cost worksheet

For each shortlisted vendor, on *your* document mix and volume:

1. Get the per-page list price and the bulk tier you qualify for.

2. Estimate per-call cost on your actual document size distribution — not an

"average" document.

3. Measure the success rate on your documents and compute

per-correct-extraction cost.

4. Estimate the cost per error, weighted by the vendor's error-mode mix —

refusals are cheap, silent wrong-number postings are not.

5. Total cost of ownership = per-correct cost × volume + (error rate × cost per

error × volume) + integration/engineering cost.

The vendor with the lowest per-page price is frequently *not* the lowest total

cost once steps 3–5 are done. That is the entire point of the framework.
