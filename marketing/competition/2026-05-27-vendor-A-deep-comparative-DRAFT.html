Trainable-IDP-with-an-API-veneer vs API-native fine-tuned — the head-to-head, honestly

Most "document-AI comparison" blogs pick one vendor name, one accuracy

percentage, and one screenshot, and rank competitors against them. We are

going to do the opposite. We are going to compare **approach against

approach** — trainable-IDP-with-an-API-veneer against `API-native

fine-tuned` — on five axes a finance buyer actually evaluates, and we

will name only the side of the comparison whose numbers we publish.

The named comparison — vendor A — lives in our internal companion at

content/competitor-studies/<vendor>/STUDY-2026-05-27.md. That artifact

is gated and not externally distributed. This post is the public half of

the same study, and we keep it honest by category-labeling the vendor and

naming only the things we can anchor to a public artifact.

If you are a buyer evaluating a category-leading IDP vendor against

HyperAPI, this post tells you the structure of the comparison. The

gated study fills in the names. The 2026-05-26 stack-level comparative

companion (comparative-blogs/2026-05-26-deep-comparative-document-ai-stack.md)

gives you the seven-primitive, fourteen-cell framework this post sits

under.

1. The two approaches, named by category

We need terms. From the 2026-05-26 stack-level comparative, the category

breaks into five approach families:

1. Rules-and-templates IDP — per-layout template definitions, regex

plus position. The classic OCR-plus-rules stack.

2. Legacy OCR-plus-rules layers — character recognition engine bolted

to a workflow rules layer; mature, on-prem, regulated back office.

3. LLM-only naïve extraction — send a PDF to a frontier

vision-language model, ask in prose for the fields, parse the JSON.

4. Trainable-IDP-with-an-API-veneer — a vendor portal where the

customer labels example documents and the system trains a

per-customer model, then exposes it behind an "extraction" endpoint.

Better than fixed templates, weaker than category-trained models on

rare layouts. The end-to-end "extraction" surface is the API; the

internal pipeline (parse / classify / split / etc.) is not addressable

separately.

5. API-native, fine-tuned, finance-trained — small open-source

backbones, fine-tuned on finance documents, exposed as

primitive-resolved endpoints so a buyer addresses each primitive

separately rather than as one extraction blob. This is where HyperAPI

sits.

This post is the head-to-head between family 4 (the named category

leader on our gated side, the vendor against whom this study was scoped)

and family 5 (HyperAPI). The gated companion identifies the family-4

vendor by name; this post does not.

2.1 Axis 1 — model backbone disclosure

A buyer asks: *what is your model?*

  • Trainable-IDP-with-an-API-veneer. Closed stack. The public

surface, as we can read it on disk, characterizes the model as a

proprietary "AI" trained per customer; the backbone is not disclosed.

[PUBLIC-SURFACE PENDING-VERIFICATION].

  • API-native fine-tuned (HyperAPI). Open-source backbones,

fine-tuned by Hyperbots (Hyperbots IP). LoRA recipe is in the

SAVIOR-Bench paper's Appendix A. Both fine-tuned [FT] rows and

zero-shot rows are tagged inline in every results table — the

fine-tune disclosure is the first paragraph of the paper's Abstract,

not a buried footnote.

The honest reading: the difference is not "open beats closed."

Plenty of closed stacks ship excellent products. The difference is

disclosure discipline. A buyer who needs to audit "what runs in

production on my documents" will get further on the right column than

on the left.

2.2 Axis 2 — API decomposition

A buyer asks: *what is the API surface?*

  • Trainable-IDP-with-an-API-veneer. End-to-end "extraction"

endpoint with vertical templates and a labeling portal upstream.

Match, validate, classify, split, augment, reason — present, but as

features of one box, not separately addressable. `[PUBLIC-SURFACE

PENDING-VERIFICATION]` for the specific endpoint enumeration on

this vendor.

  • API-native fine-tuned (HyperAPI). Primitive-resolved:

parse, parse-x, parse-intent, split, classify, extract,

extract-omni, redact, detect, validate, reconcile,

bundle. Each is its own endpoint; each has its own pricing line;

each has its own failure mode. A buyer can plug extract upstream

of their ERP without adopting reason.

The honest reading: both shapes are valid. A buyer who wants "one

endpoint, one JSON" gets faster time-to-first-extract on a templated

flow. A buyer whose stack already has match, validate, and audit-trace

modules wants to address extract and reason separately and not pay

for a re-implementation of what their ERP already does. The shape

question is structural, not a quality question — and the gated study

walks through which shape suits which buyer.

3. The honest negative findings — both sides

A comparative blog that only reports the side where the author wins

is a marketing post. We will not write one. Two negative findings

sit on the HyperAPI side of this comparison and we publish them up

front:

3.1 Our extract cell on dataset_95_granular is below three measured peers

On dataset_95_granular (95 invoices, factory-reproduced), the

Hyperbots IDP chain (parse + extract) lands at 0.3573 micro-F1,

*below* Gemini 2.5 Flash (0.7132), Gemini 2.5 Pro (0.6195), Azure DI

prebuilt-invoice (0.6078), and Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite (0.5004) on the

same dataset. The failure mode is identified: two long invoices

([55, 68]) hit the 300-second server-side extract timeout — the same

long-invoice failure mode that dropped Gemini Pro on [42, 51] and

Flash on [2, 3, 41, 42, 51]. We published the 0.3573 number in the

CONSOLIDATED-150 delta on 2026-05-25. We are working the long-invoice

timeout; we are not hiding the cell.

3.2 Our fine-tune edge over the best zero-shot is narrow

Parse-intent +0.0276 F1 over GPT-4o; OCR +0.0061 recall over Textract

and Azure-DI. Both are real, both have non-overlapping reads against

the rest of the panel — and both are smaller than the "10x" the

category would imply if the marketing copy were the only data source.

We publish that because the *moat* is not the model; the moat is the

**corpus, the primitive decomposition, the rerunnable harness, the

audit discipline, and the open pricing line**. The 2026-07-01 post in

this series (the corpus-and-loop argument) goes deeper. For this post,

the honest read is: the model is a piece of the answer, not the whole

answer.

A reader who has read both this paragraph and the gated companion has

seen the comparison in both directions. That is the whole point.

4. The seven primitives, walked against this comparison

The 2026-05-26 stack-level comparative gave the seven-primitive split

(parse, classify, split, match, validate, augment,

reason) and the fourteen cells of work (seven primitives × two

process families, P2P and O2C). Against this category leader,

primitive by primitive, the structural question is the same one:

  • parse — is the parser addressable separately, with its own

metric (field-level P/R/F1) and its own failure-mode bucketing

(the 7-bucket error taxonomy in 01-measuring-extraction-accuracy.md)?

  • classify — is calibration (D9 in the deep-study framework)

measurable on the surface, or is "accuracy" the only available

metric?

  • split — is bundle-PDF splitting a first-class primitive or a

preprocessing detail the buyer is expected to solve?

  • match — is 3-way / 2-way / N-way match an addressable

primitive with explicit tolerance pins as configuration, or is the

tolerance policy hand-coded per customer?

  • validate — is the rule catalog auditable as a first-class

object, or is the rule a prompt-string?

  • augment — are master-data lookups built in as a first-class

call with a freshness SLA, or stitched together per integration?

  • reason — does the system return a decision *and* a

field-citing justification trace, scored against a *distribution* of

human decisions rather than a single right answer?

For HyperAPI, every primitive answers "yes — addressable, named,

measured (or honestly pending)." For the family-4 category leader,

each answer is [PUBLIC-SURFACE PENDING-VERIFICATION] until the

follow-up beat lands. The gated companion records the URLs to fetch

and the depth-pattern questions to apply, ten per primitive.

The honest reading: the differentiator is not the per-primitive

accuracy — it is the addressability. A buyer who needs to

instrument match and validate separately gets a different

purchase from a stack that exposes them separately than from one

that buries them inside an "extraction" endpoint.

5. The buyer's three questions — and the honest answers

We will not pretend this post answers the buyer's question for them.

But three questions a head-to-head deal will surface, with the honest

HyperAPI answer:

5.1 "Show me your accuracy."

Per apis.hyperbots.com, product accuracy is what the page says it
is. Beyond product copy: our public benchmark — SAVIOR-Bench v1, 509
documents — places the fine-tuned hyperapi-parse-intent at F1
0.8328 / WER 0.2589 against 23 systems, with a +0.0276 F1 edge over
the strongest documented zero-shot. We say that out loud because the
alternative is the kind of headline number that doesn't survive your
second invoice. The W6 audit re-derived 0 of 23 SAVIOR cells under
our own harness — we publish that too, because if we cannot
reproduce our own paper, we want to know first.

5.2 "What's your API surface?"

Primitive-resolved. parse, parse-x, parse-intent, split,
classify, extract, extract-omni, redact, detect,
validate, reconcile, bundle. Each is its own endpoint; each
has its own pricing line. You can plug extract upstream of your
ERP without adopting reason. The trainable-IDP family ships one
end-to-end endpoint with vertical templates — both shapes are
valid; the right shape depends on whether you want one box or a
toolkit.

5.3 "What does it cost?"

$0.05/page published, volume sized on a call. The pricing URL is
the pricing — there is no hidden tier. For a head-to-head against a
trainable-IDP-with-an-API-veneer vendor whose public pricing
requires a sales call, this is itself a credibility line: a buyer
can plan their budget without an NDA.

For an ML practitioner: read the SAVIOR-Bench v1 paper, run the

12-cell SEED leaderboard on your laptop, then point your evaluation

at the seven-primitive decomposition rather than a single F1. The

2026-05-22 measurement-methodology post is the rubric.

backbone disclosure, API decomposition, published benchmark surface,

reproducibility, pricing transparency — and accept "we don't publish

that" as an answer. The vendors who answer cleanly on three of five

are the ones to shortlist; the ones who answer with adjectives on

all five are the ones to deprioritize.

For a CIO / finance-tech executive: notice the structural

asymmetry. One side has a published benchmark, a published audit, a

published pricing line, and a primitive-resolved API. The other side

honest measurement gap on our side or a posture difference on

theirs, and the right buyer rubric makes the difference visible
