Document AI for finance — the stack-level comparison, honestly

Most "document-AI comparison" posts pick one number — accuracy on

invoices — and rank vendors against it. That picture is the wrong shape.

Finance teams do not buy "extraction." They buy a stack: a pipeline that

turns a folder of mixed PDFs into approved postings in a general ledger,

with an audit trail. The pipeline has at least seven distinct jobs of

work, and each job is its own measurement problem. Collapsing them into

"99% accuracy" is how vendor pages talk past finance buyers.

This post is the comparative draft for the deep-study framework that

maps the document-AI stack honestly. We worked the map across two

finance domains — procure-to-pay (P2P) and order-to-cash (O2C) —

and seven primitives — **parse, classify, split, match, validate,

augment, reason** — for a total of fourteen cells of work. The full

framework lives behind an internal landing page; this post is the public

narrative for the comparison.

Read the gated study: the full deep-study framework, with the
nine-section spine, the fourteen-dimension D-axis, the eight-row
data-status legend, and a per-cell plan for all fourteen cells, is at
niyatic.github.io/content/nanonets-framework.
It is password-gated and internal-facing. This post links to it; the
categorical comparison in this post does not name competitors.

A note on what we are and are not claiming. The framework is a

measurement plan, not a measurement run. Every result cell in it

carries [PLACEHOLDER] or [PENDING-MEASUREMENT]. We narrate the stack

qualitatively here — approaches, failure modes, what to demand of a

vendor — and we cite numbers only from primary measurement artifacts

elsewhere on disk. Where we cannot honestly run a comparison, we say so.

That negative finding is part of the asset.

1. The honest framing — seven primitives, two processes, fourteen cells

The first move is to stop treating a document-AI stack as one box.

Borrowing the framework's structure, the work decomposes cleanly:

The seven primitives.

PrimitiveThe one-line job
parseTurn raw bytes into structured fields, line items and table cells.
classifyAssign each input document to its doc-type label.
splitCut a bundle PDF into logical sub-documents at the correct page boundaries.
matchTie one document to others (3-way / 2-way / N-way) by IDs, line items, totals, dates, parties.
validateCheck extracted and matched data against business rules: totals, tax, duplicate, date sanity, party allow-list.
augmentEnrich extracted data via lookups: master data, tax registries, PO-line catalogues, FX rates, holiday calendars.
reasonDecide exceptions — "approve", "hold", "route-to", "ask for" — with a justification trace.

The two process families.

  • P2P (procure-to-pay). Purchase orders, goods-receipt notes,

supplier invoices, bills of lading, packing slips, credit notes,

debit notes, freight invoices.

  • O2C (order-to-cash). Sales orders, sales invoices, advance

shipping notices, customer credit memos, dunning notices, statements

of account, remittance advice, dispute correspondence.

Seven primitives times two domains is fourteen cells of work. The

deep-study framework has a dedicated plan for each, with its own

in-scope dimensions, sample-design parameters, stratification axes, and

falsifiable hypotheses. The plan files live under

content/deep-study-framework/plans/ in the gated repo.

The honest payoff of this structure: a vendor that "does invoices" may

be excellent at parse and weak at match and silent on reason. A

finance buyer needs to know *which cell* the vendor is strong on,

because each cell has a different cost profile and a different production

failure mode. Collapsing them obscures the very thing the buyer needs to

see.

2. What the category looks like today — by approach, not by name

This is the section where most comparative posts go off the rails: they

list vendor names and assign scores. We will not do that here. The named

comparison lives in the gated study; the public narrative compares

approaches, because approach is what determines where a stack will

break in production.

We see five recognisable approaches in the category. Each handles the

seven primitives differently, and each has a characteristic failure

mode.

2.1 Rules-and-templates IDP

The classic approach. A human (or a tool-assisted human) defines a

template per layout — "the invoice number lives in this rectangle, the

total in that one" — and the engine pulls fields by position plus

regex.

  • parse: strong on the in-template vendor; brittle the moment a

layout drifts. New vendor → new template, every time.

  • classify: usually a rules layer over keywords or doc-shape.
  • split: separator-page or regex heuristics; struggles on bundled

PDFs with no separator.

  • match: typically a rules engine layered on top, separate from the

extractor.

  • validate: native strength — rules are what these systems do.
  • augment: pluggable via lookups, but lookup management is its own

ops problem.

  • reason: not really in scope; the system raises exceptions for a

human queue rather than deciding them.

Where it breaks. Vendor sprawl. The day-two cost of a

templates-stack is template maintenance against the long tail of

suppliers — typically the 20-percent of suppliers that send 80-percent

of the layout variance.

2.2 Legacy OCR-plus-rules layers

A character-recognition engine bolted to a rules layer, usually

on-prem. Mature, audit-friendly, and broadly deployed in regulated

back offices.

  • parse: OCR is the strong link; field assignment downstream of OCR

is the weak one.

  • classify: keyword and shape heuristics; works on a small,

enumerated doc-type set.

  • split: present, but usually configured per workflow.
  • match: lives outside the OCR product, in the workflow engine.
  • validate: lives in the workflow engine; mature on tax and duplicate

rules.

  • augment: requires ETL into the workflow tool.
  • reason: not a primitive these stacks own.

Where it breaks. Layout shift, low-DPI scans, mobile capture, and

multilingual flows. The OCR engine treats every page as a fresh

recognition problem; it does not learn from yesterday's vendor.

2.3 LLM-only naïve extraction

The newest entrant. Send a PDF to a frontier vision-language model,

ask in prose for the fields you want, parse the JSON.

  • parse: surprisingly strong on the easy cases; surprisingly bad on

the hard ones (long-tail invoices, spanning-header tables, multi-leg

bills of lading).

  • classify: zero-shot is usable for coarse labels.
  • split: weak — the model has no native sense of "document

boundary"; it sees page tokens.

  • match: not a fit. Match is a database operation against a corpus

of POs and GRNs; an LLM-only flow has nowhere to put that corpus.

  • validate: doable in prompt, but auditability is poor — the rule

becomes a string the model decides whether to honour.

  • augment: requires plumbing out to lookups; the LLM is not the

master-data store.

  • reason: viable at the surface, weak in the trace — getting the

decision out is easy, getting a faithful, citation-bearing

justification trace is hard.

Where it breaks. Schema-hallucination, line-item drift on long

invoices, and the bill at the end of the month. Per-token pricing

scales with document size, not with extracted value.

2.4 Template-based extraction stacks ("trainable IDP")

The middle of the market: a vendor portal where a customer labels

example documents and the system learns a per-customer model. Better

than fixed templates, weaker than category-trained models on rare

layouts.

  • parse: strong on the customer's own corpus, weaker on

out-of-distribution suppliers.

  • classify: usually well-served; the system learns the customer's

doc-type set.

  • split: typically a learned classifier on page-pair features.
  • match: separate module; works against the customer's ERP.
  • validate: rules layer; integrates with the trained model's

confidence.

  • augment: integrations to the customer's master data.
  • reason: rule-driven, sometimes ML-assisted on routing.

Where it breaks. Cold-start. New customer → labelling burden →

weeks-to-months before the model is competitive on rare-but-important

strata. And the model's edge is the customer's corpus, which means it

does not transfer.

2.5 API-native, fine-tuned, finance-trained (where HyperAPI sits)

This is the approach Hyperbots ships. **Small open-source backbones,

fine-tuned by Hyperbots on finance documents (Hyperbots IP)**, exposed

as primitive-resolved endpoints — parse, parse-x, parse-intent,

split, classify, extract, extract-omni, redact, detect,

validate, reconcile, bundle — so a buyer can address each

primitive separately rather than as one extraction blob.

  • parse, parse-x, parse-intent: trained on financial-document

variance; the fine-tune edge per the comparative study is "real but

narrow" — top-of-panel parse-intent F1 and OCR recall by small

margins on the SAVIOR-Bench v1 panel, and losing on precision and

layout against the strongest zero-shot VLM systems. We say that

honestly in the cell plans rather than imply a clean blowout.

  • classify: same fine-tuned backbone; doc-type panel covers the P2P

and O2C families above.

  • split: bundle splitting against learned page-pair features.
  • match (reconcile): 2-way / 3-way / N-way with explicit

tolerance pins, FX cross-rate handling, and reason-code agreement

as a first-class metric.

  • validate: rule layer with per-rule coverage and false-block-rate

as a tracked metric.

  • augment: pluggable master-data, FX and tax-registry lookups; FX

freshness scored.

  • reason: a decision endpoint that returns a decision *and* a

field-citing justification trace, scored against a distribution of

human decisions (not a single right answer), with

off-distribution-decision rate tracked separately.

Where it does not yet beat the field. Several of the dimensions

the framework introduces (confidence calibration, throughput, visual

grounding, straight-through processing, per-doc latency) are not

enumerated in the two industry taxonomies the framework crosswalks

against; they are framework-introduced study gaps. The honest stance is

that nobody — including us — has authoritative published numbers

across the full seven-primitive surface on a pinned, rerunnable

basis. That is the gap the deep-study library is built to close.

What the approach comparison gives the buyer

The right shape of comparison is not "vendor A vs vendor B at

extraction." It is "approach X vs approach Y across the seven

primitives, in your domain." For a finance buyer, the questions that

fall out of this framing are different from the questions a single

accuracy ranking would surface:

  • On match, does the candidate own a tolerance policy as

configuration, or hand-code it per customer?

  • On validate, is the rule layer enumerable and audit-friendly, or

is the rule a prompt-string?

  • On reason, does the candidate return a decision *and* a citation

trace, or only a decision?

  • On augment, where do the master-data lookups live, and what is the

freshness SLA?

Those questions cut across all five approaches without needing a

single ranking number.

3. The fourteen cells, walked

The framework's contribution is not the seven-primitive split — others

have written variants of that. The contribution is the **per-cell

plan**: for each of the fourteen cells, the same nine-section spine,

the same data-status legend, the same Δ-rule (non-overlapping 95%

confidence intervals or it is a tie band), and the same rerunnable-

command form. The cells differ only in workload, dimension

applicability, stratification axes, and per-primitive limits.

Below is the comparative narrative cell by cell. We deliberately do not

introduce numbers in this section; the cells' result columns are

[PENDING-MEASUREMENT] in the framework on disk and we are not going

to invent them here. We narrate approach, failure mode, and what

distinguishes API-native fine-tuned from the four other approaches.

3.1 Parse (P2P) — the long tail of supplier invoices

The dominant dimensions are field coverage and table fidelity. P2P

parse has eight document families — purchase order, goods-receipt

note, supplier invoice, bill of lading, packing slip, credit note,

debit note, freight invoice — each with its own field schema. The

hard cases are not the clean digital supplier-invoice PDF; they are

the photographed delivery docket, the bilingual carrier doc, the

multi-leg bill of lading with hazmat codes, and the long-tail

invoice with spanning-header tables.

A template-based stack handles the high-volume vendors and breaks on

the long tail. An LLM-only flow handles short invoices well and

loses line items as the table grows. A fine-tuned, financial-domain

backbone is built for the long tail specifically — the fine-tune

edge, per the SAVIOR-Bench v1 panel measured by the benchmark

factory, lives precisely on the line-item, OCR-stressed strata.

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/parse-p2p.md.

3.2 Parse (O2C) — remittance and the line that does not exist

O2C parse is dominated by remittance advice — the document that

says "this $12,400 wire covers invoices A, B and C, less a $300

short-pay." Remittance has no canonical layout; it arrives as PDF,

email body, fax, spreadsheet, image, or check-stub. The hard work is

turning a free-form payment narrative into a structured invoice list

plus reason-coded short-pays.

Template-based stacks struggle here because there is no template to

build against. LLM-only is workable but, again, the line-by-line

fidelity drops on long lists. The fine-tuned backbone is trained on

the variance pattern.

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/parse-o2c.md.

3.3 Classify (P2P) — twelve doc types and the cost of confusion

Classifying among PO / GRN / invoice / BoL / packing slip / credit

note / debit note / freight is not hard *on average*. It is hard on

the confusion pairs: a freight invoice that looks like a supplier

invoice; a debit note that looks like a credit note with a sign

flip. The right metric is macro-F1 plus per-class precision / recall

plus a confusion-matrix entropy view, not a single "classification

accuracy."

The classify primitive is also where calibration (D9 in the

framework) starts to matter. A miscalibrated classifier with high

average accuracy is worse than a slightly-lower-accuracy classifier

that knows when it is uncertain — because the calibrated one routes

the right cases to human review.

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/classify-p2p.md.

3.4 Classify (O2C) — remittance vs statement-of-account

The signature confusion on the O2C side is remittance-advice vs

statement-of-account. They overlap in surface form; they have very

different downstream pipelines. The cell plan stratifies on exactly

that confusion pair as its hardest band, and tracks per-class P/R

rather than the global accuracy.

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/classify-o2c.md.

3.5 Split (P2P) — bundle PDFs without separators

Split is the primitive that gets the least attention and the one that

silently bottlenecks production. A bundle PDF — fifty pages of mixed

documents emailed in by a supplier — is the normal case, not the

edge case. Splitting it into the right sub-documents at the right

page boundaries is what unlocks parse and classify downstream.

The dominant dimensions are boundary-F1 (did we cut at the right

place?) and page-attribution accuracy (did the right pages end up

with the right sub-document?). Template-based stacks rely on

separator pages; LLM-only flows have no native split concept; fine-

tuned classifiers learn page-pair features.

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/split-p2p.md.

3.6 Split (O2C) — the remit-and-check pair

The O2C split case is the lock-box scan: a remittance advice and its

accompanying check, scanned together, sometimes with a deposit slip

in the middle. Pairing them correctly is the cell's dominant problem.

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/split-o2c.md.

3.7 Match (P2P) — three-way match is where the money is

match-p2p is the signature P2P primitive. The three-way match —

supplier_invoice ↔ purchase_order ↔ goods_receipt_note — is what

finance teams are paying for. Two-way matches happen too (no goods

received yet), and N-way matches against blanket POs or multi-GRN

fulfilments are common.

The right metrics are per-leg precision / recall / F1 across the

three legs, end-to-end 3-way match rate, dollar coverage (sum of

invoice totals correctly matched, divided by total invoice value),

false-block rate (precision of rejection), and reason-code agreement

(when the system holds on a tolerance excursion, does it cite the

same reason a human would?).

Tolerance policy is part of the methodology, not a free parameter.

The cell pins quantity, price, total and date tolerances per

edition; cross-currency comparisons use a pinned FX table snapshot.

Most approaches under-spec this. A templates stack treats match as a

downstream rules problem and ships it to a workflow engine. An

LLM-only flow has nowhere to put the PO and GRN corpus. The API-

native, fine-tuned approach handles match as a first-class primitive

with explicit tolerance pins.

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/match-p2p.md.

3.8 Match (O2C) — cash application

match-o2c is cash application: tie an incoming payment to the

right open invoices, handle short-pays, partial applications, and

remittance-driven multi-invoice cover-offs. The dominant business

metric is straight-through-processing rate (D12): of the incoming

payments, what fraction the system can fully resolve with no human

touch. That is what the AR team buys.

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/match-o2c.md.

3.9 Validate (P2P) — the rule layer as a first-class artifact

Validate is the rule-coverage primitive. The metrics are rule-coverage

F1 (per rule, what fraction of cases that should have triggered did),

false-block rate (cases the system holds that humans would

release), and calibration against human override. The honest

question for any approach is: can a finance team audit the rule

catalog as a first-class object? A rules-and-templates IDP scores

well here. An LLM-only flow scores poorly — the rule is whatever the

prompt says, and the model decides whether to honour it.

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/validate-p2p.md.

3.10 Validate (O2C) — aging and credit policy

The O2C validate cell covers aging-bucket assignment, credit-policy

checks, dispute-window logic. Same shape, different rules.

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/validate-o2c.md.

3.11 Augment (P2P) — master-data lookups and FX freshness

Augment is the lookup primitive: vendor master data, tax registry,

PO-line catalogue, FX rate, holiday calendar. The right metrics are

enrichment hit-rate, lookup precision, freshness (how stale is

the FX rate?), and FX-error rate (mis-currency or wrong cross).

Approach matters here less than plumbing. The question is whether

the augment primitive is built in as a first-class call with a

freshness SLA, or stitched together per integration.

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/augment-p2p.md.

3.12 Augment (O2C) — open-invoice retrieval

On the O2C side, augment is dominated by open-invoice retrieval —

pulling the customer's open invoice list correctly, in the right

order, at remittance application time.

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/augment-o2c.md.

3.13 Reason (P2P) — exceptions with a justification trace

reason is the hardest primitive, and the ground truth is a

distribution rather than a single correct answer. Three

independent human reviewers per case; the modal decision is the

high-confidence ground truth; 2-of-3 agreement is medium-confidence;

full-disagreement cases are explicitly excluded from headline

metrics and reported separately.

A key metric specific to reason: off-distribution decision rate —

the fraction of cases where the system picks an option *no human

picked*. Off-distribution decisions are flagged and never counted as

correct.

The justification trace is its own scoring problem: does the trace

cite the fields, rules and lookups that actually drove the human

modal decision? Or does it cite fields that do not exist in the

document?

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/reason-p2p.md.

3.14 Reason (O2C) — collections and dispute routing

The O2C reason cell covers collections routing, dispute escalation,

and credit-policy-driven decisions. Same distributional-ground-truth

methodology; different decision surface.

Cell plan: content/deep-study-framework/plans/reason-o2c.md.

This is the section most comparative posts will not write. We do.

azure-di-invoice-study — which itself ships explicit, the

5. A note on what "accuracy" means in product copy

Product accuracy claims for HyperAPI are published only **per

apis.hyperbots.com**. Benchmark figures referenced anywhere in this

post (such as the SAVIOR-Bench v1 panel) are model OCR / parse

performance under a stated panel, not product accuracy. The two are

different objects and we keep them lexically separate to avoid the

inflation pattern that does the category no favours.

Likewise, **the fine-tunes are open-source backbones fine-tuned by

Hyperbots (Hyperbots IP)** — we do not claim closed-source ownership

of open weights, and the cell plans carry that disclosure in their

§3.6 independence-and-disclosure block, alongside the affiliation

declaration.

6. What to do with this — for the buyer, for the practitioner

For a finance buyer, the practical move is: stop asking vendors

for "accuracy" and start asking which of the seven primitives the

vendor is responsible for, what each primitive's failure mode looks

like in your domain, and which dimensions of the framework the vendor

will commit to measuring on your documents. The buyer's rubric in the

existing comparative-blogs series (post 10) operationalises that.

For an ML or platform practitioner, the move is: pick one cell of

the fourteen, take its plan file as a template, and run the cell on

your own dataset against your own systems. The plan files specify

every input the run needs — sample design, stratification, metrics,

scoring rubric, evaluation controls, the literal rerunnable command —

and the eight-row data-status legend lets the result be honest about

which cells are measured and which are pending. The plan IS the

study; converting placeholders to measured numbers, with a pinned

dataset SHA256, is what publishes the cell.

For a CIO or finance-tech executive, the move is: ask the

candidate vendors to show their per-primitive resolution. A vendor

that can only show "extraction accuracy" is selling one primitive

(parse) and waving at the other six. That is not a stack — that is a

feature.
