Multi-currency and multi-language invoice accuracy — a coverage-gap analysis

A US-only invoice corpus is a comfortable benchmark. A global AP function does

not have one. The moment your document mix includes EUR invoices with European

number formatting, a Japanese vendor's bilingual invoice, or a Brazilian nota

fiscal, a whole class of failures appears that an English-clean-digital benchmark

never measured. This post maps that coverage gap and how to measure it.

The two distinct problems

"Multi-language / multi-currency" is actually two separate failure surfaces.

Problem 1 — numeric and locale formatting

This is a *numeric_format_error* in the error taxonomy: the number is recognized

but the locale or sign is mishandled.

  • Decimal/grouping inversion. 1.234,56 in a German locale is one-thousand;

a US-locale parser reads it as 1.234. EUR 1.234,56 mis-parsed is a

3-orders-of-magnitude posting error.

  • Currency symbol and code. R$, kr, 元, CHF, and the position of the

symbol (before vs after the amount) all vary. A model that strips "the currency

symbol" assuming a leading $ drops the wrong character elsewhere.

  • Negative-number conventions. ($125.00), 125.00-, -125.00, and a red

font with no sign are all "negative" in different document traditions.

  • Date formats. 03/04/2026 is March 4 or April 3 depending on locale. A

date field that silently picks one is wrong half the time.

Problem 2 — script and language recognition

This is closer to an ocr_character_error plus a field-identification problem:

  • Non-Latin scripts — CJK, Cyrillic, Arabic (right-to-left), Devanagari —

each is a distinct OCR challenge.

  • Field-label identification across languages. The model has to know that

"Rechnungsnummer", "Numéro de facture", "請求書番号", and "Invoice number" are

the same field. A pipeline that field-matches on English keyword strings fails

silently on a translated invoice.

  • Bilingual documents — many invoices carry two languages on one page;

reading-order and region segmentation get harder.

Why this is a benchmark coverage gap, not just a model gap

The deeper point: most published accuracy numbers were never *measured* on

non-English, non-USD documents. The document-quality stratification convention

makes multilingual an explicit stratum — a document whose primary language is

not English, carrying a language code, tagged *before* any model runs. If a

vendor's benchmark has no multilingual stratum, their headline accuracy number

simply does not describe your German or Japanese invoices. The number is not

wrong; it is *silent* on your hardest documents.

How to measure multi-currency / multi-language accuracy properly

1. Stratify by language and by currency-locale before running anything.

Report per-stratum F1 with n and 95% CI.

2. Score numeric fields with a numeric-tolerance metric that is

locale-aware — 1.234,56 and 1,234.56 must both resolve to the same value

before comparison.

3. Report a dedicated numeric_format_error rate per locale.

4. Test date disambiguation explicitly — DD/MM vs MM/DD is a coin-flip you

must measure, not assume.

5. Include bilingual and RTL documents as their own strata.

6. Never blend a multilingual result into a global aggregate as the headline —

the stratification convention puts the blended number in an appendix.

What to ask a vendor

  • "Show me your multilingual stratum and your per-currency-locale numbers,

with n."

  • "What is your numeric_format_error rate on European-formatted amounts?"
  • "How do you disambiguate DD/MM vs MM/DD dates — and what is the measured error

rate?"

  • "Which scripts have you actually benchmarked, on how many documents?"

If the answer to any of these is a blended global number, the vendor has a

coverage gap they have not measured — and on a global document mix, that gap is

your production incident.
