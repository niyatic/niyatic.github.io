There's an assumption baked into most model-selection decisions: the premium tier is the accurate tier, and the cheap tier is the one you settle for. We ran the test on finance invoices. The assumption broke.

The result

Full-n extract benchmark on dataset_95 (95 invoices, factory-reproduced 2026-05-25), invoice_extract_micro_f1:

Modelmicro-F1line-count exact$/docdocs extracted
Gemini 2.5 Flash0.71320.7333$0.01090 / 95
Gemini 2.5 Pro0.61950.6452$0.03393 / 95
Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite0.50040.5895$0.00195 / 95

Flash beat Pro by +0.0937 micro-F1 — at roughly one-third the per-document cost. The "premium" model was both more expensive and less accurate on this corpus.

Why this isn't a fluke (and the one honest caveat)

The natural objection: maybe Pro just attempted harder documents. Worth checking — and partly true. Pro extracted 93 of 95 documents vs Flash's 90 of 95, so Pro did get 3 more across the line. But within the docs each actually completed, Flash's per-document quality was higher: Flash leads on both micro-F1 (0.7132 vs 0.6195) and line-count-exact accuracy (0.7333 vs 0.6452). The cheaper model wasn't winning on a technicality — it was extracting cleaner records.

So the honest read is not "Pro is bad." It's: *on this finance-invoice corpus, the premium model's extra capability did not translate into extraction accuracy, and it cost 3× more to find that out.*

The part we don't hide

This is a Gemini-vs-Gemini finding — it's about model selection, not about us winning. For the record, on this same dataset_95, the current Hyperbots IDP chain lands at 0.3573 micro-F1 — below all three Gemini tiers. We publish that because the credibility of the finding depends on us not cherry-picking. The point of D-01 isn't "we're better." It's "your intuition about which model to use is a hypothesis, not a fact — and on finance documents it's frequently wrong."

What a buyer (or an ML lead) should take from this

1. Don't pay the premium tax on faith. On your documents, run the cheaper tier head-to-head before defaulting to the flagship. Here the cheaper tier was both better *and* 3× cheaper — that's a real budget line.

2. Separate "attempt rate" from "accuracy." Pro attempted more docs but extracted worse ones. A model that returns *something* on 100% of inputs can still be the wrong choice if the something is wrong. Measure both axes.

3. Per-corpus, not per-leaderboard. A model's general-benchmark ranking does not predict its accuracy on *your* invoice mix. The only number that matters is the one you measure on your own documents.

4. This is why we benchmark every model per corpus rather than assume — the same discipline that produced this finding is the one behind the SAVIOR-Bench / SEED leaderboard.
