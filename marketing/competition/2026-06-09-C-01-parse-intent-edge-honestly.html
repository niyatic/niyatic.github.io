Most vendor blogs you'll read this quarter will quote a "10× better than the leading LLM" line. We benchmarked our fine-tune against the leading LLM, on a finance-document corpus, on a primitive most buyers care about. The honest number is +0.0276 F1.

That's the headline. It's a small number. We published it anyway, because the credibility of every other claim we make depends on it.

The result

Parse-intent (intent + structured-field reading on financial documents) — SAVIOR-Bench v1:

SystemF1WER
hyperapi-parse-intent [FT]0.83280.2589
GPT-4o (zero-shot)0.80520.4232
Delta+0.0276 F1−0.1643 WER

Two things to notice.

First, the F1 lead is small — 27 thousandths. We are not claiming a magnitude blowout. We are claiming a measured, modest, sourced edge on this primitive on this corpus.

Second, the WER drop is large — 0.16 absolute, roughly a 39% relative cut in word-error-rate. The same fine-tune that nudges F1 up by 0.0276 cuts the transcription error rate by almost two-fifths. Those two numbers are about different things — F1 is about *whether the right fields ended up in the right slots*, WER is about *whether the words inside them are correct* — and the gap between them is the architectural story.

Why a small F1 lead with a big WER drop is the interesting finding

If a zero-shot frontier model is already at 0.8052 F1 on this primitive, you can't move the F1 by much without a different *kind* of investment. The fine-tune does that — but the place it shows up most isn't field-routing accuracy (F1), it's *transcription accuracy inside the field* (WER). For finance documents this matters: getting the right total in the right slot but transcribing the *digits* wrong is a downstream-pipeline poison. WER halving is the kind of edge a controller actually notices, even when the headline F1 number sounds modest.

The honest part — where the fine-tune loses

This isn't "we win at everything." Two losses, published honestly:

  • On the OCR task in the same benchmark panel, zero-shot frontier beats our fine-tunes on precision and PaIRS. GPT-4o leads OCR precision at 0.9322 (vs our 7B fine-tune at 0.9157); PaddleOCR-VL-1.5 leads PaIRS at 0.8766 (vs our 0.7066, a 0.17 gap). Our small-backbone fine-tunes lead OCR recall (+0.0061) and parse-intent F1 (+0.0276); they lose precision and layout to the best zero-shot systems. Real but narrow, in both directions.
  • On a different task — full-pipeline invoice extract on dataset_95 — the current Hyperbots IDP chain lands at 0.3573 micro-F1, below Gemini Flash's 0.7132. End-to-end extract is a structurally harder task than parse-intent, and our chain has not yet closed that gap. We publish that too. The bet isn't "we already won the corpus"; the bet is that decomposition + per-primitive measurement is the path to closing those gaps in a way an end-to-end black box cannot.

So what is the bet, if the edge is small?

The bet is not the model. The bet is:

1. The corpus is the moat. A small open backbone fine-tuned on a deep, expert-annotated finance corpus (509 docs, IAA 0.761, five corpora; SAVIOR-Bench v1) generalizes within finance documents in a way a general-purpose VLM does not. The +0.0276 F1 / −0.1643 WER on parse-intent is what that corpus discipline buys, today.

2. Ownable economics. A small fine-tuned model (Qwen2.5-VL-3B/7B with a LoRA recipe — rank 32, α 64, lr 1e-4 vision / 5e-4 language; SAVIOR-Bench App. A) is cheap to serve at finance-throughput volumes (drafted ~$0.19 per 1,000 successful calls on A100-40GB, infrastructure-derived). Per-token frontier inference is not.

3. Primitive-resolved measurability. Because the pipeline is decomposed into seven primitives (parse / classify / split / match / validate / augment / reason), the +0.0276 lives on a specific axis you can audit. The buyer can ask "did we improve at parse, classify, or extract?" and get a per-primitive answer — not "did the demo number go up?"

4. Published failure honesty. The W6 audit re-derived 0 of 23 SAVIOR cells and we shipped that finding; every claim in this post carries an attested / ci95=null / factory-reproduced label. The credibility of "+0.0276 F1" depends on this discipline, not on its magnitude.

What a buyer should take from this

1. Treat small published numbers as a credibility signal, not a magnitude one. A vendor who reports a +0.027 F1 with WER halving honestly is doing something different than a vendor who reports "10× better."

2. Match the primitive to your problem. Parse-intent is one of seven primitives in this framework — it's not invoice-extract, it's not classification, it's not validation. The right number to evaluate any vendor on is the primitive *your* workflow lives or dies on.

3. Run the head-to-head on your own corpus. The 0.0276 is on SAVIOR-Bench v1. Your finance documents are not SAVIOR-Bench. The honest test is your own.
