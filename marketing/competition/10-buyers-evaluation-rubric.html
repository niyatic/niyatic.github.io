A buyer's evaluation rubric — how to benchmark document-AI vendors

You should not buy a document-AI vendor on their benchmark. You should buy on

*your* benchmark — run on your documents, under one harness, scored one way. This

post is the rubric to do that. It is vendor-neutral by construction: run it on

a named LLM-document-parsing API, on HyperAPI (by Hyperbots), and on every shortlisted vendor identically.

Principle: bring your own held-out test set

The single most important step. Assemble your own test set of ~300–500

documents, drawn from your real document mix, with gold annotations you own.

  • Stratify it *before* running anything (see step 2). No re-slicing after seeing

results.

  • Keep it held-out — never send it to a vendor for "tuning." A vendor that

tunes on your test set has invalidated your benchmark.

  • A vendor's own published number is, at best, a reason to make the shortlist —

never a reason to skip your own run.

The rubric — eight scored sections

1. Field-level accuracy (P / R / F1)

Score every field type separately — header, line_item (row-F1), numeric,

identifier, free_text. Report precision, recall, F1 per field type. Reject

any single blended number. Demand n and a 95% CI per cell.

2. Document-quality robustness

Stratify your test set into clean_digital, scanned_high, scanned_low,

mobile_capture, multilingual, handwritten_annotated — tagged *before* the

run. Report accuracy per stratum. A vendor strong on clean digital and weak on

scanned-low has told you exactly where your backlog will fail.

3. Line-item / table fidelity

Row-level F1, not cell-level. Plus an arithmetic-reconciliation rate: on what

fraction of invoices does the line-item sum reconcile to the subtotal. Test

multi-page and wrapped-row tables explicitly. (See post 06.)

4. Multi-currency / multi-language coverage

If your document mix is global, score the multilingual stratum and per-currency

locale separately. Test DD/MM vs MM/DD date disambiguation. Measure the

numeric_format_error rate on European-formatted amounts. (See post 07.)

5. Error-mode profile

Bucket every failure into the seven-mode taxonomy (ocr_character_error,

layout_misread, reading_order_error, schema_mismatch_hallucination,

numeric_format_error, table_structure_error, refusal_no_output). The *mix*

matters: a vendor that fails by refusing is operationally safer than one that

fails by confidently posting a wrong number. (See post 09.)

6. Cost — three views

Per-page, per-call, and per-correct-extraction — all three, on your document-size

distribution. Add a cost-per-error estimate weighted by the error-mode mix from

section 5. Lowest per-page price is often not lowest total cost. (See post 09.)

7. Reproducibility and evidence tier

Ask each vendor: are your published numbers pinned and rerunnable? Is your

evidence "measured and reproduced," or "attested but not independently

reproduced"? A vendor that labels its tier honestly scores higher than one with a

glossier number and no audit trail. (See post 08.)

8. Operational fit

Latency (p50 and p99, not just average), throughput ceiling, failure-rate under

load, integration effort, data-residency and compliance posture, and whether you

can own or fine-tune the model. These are not accuracy, but they are the

deployment.

Decision rule

  • Rank within a section by CI-overlapping tie bands, not by raw point

estimates. If two vendors' CIs overlap, they are tied on that section.

  • Weight the sections by *your* priorities — an auto-posting AP team weights

precision and section 5 heavily; a RAG-ingestion use case weights layout and

throughput.

  • The winner is the best total-cost-of-ownership at your accuracy tolerance —

not the highest headline accuracy and not the lowest per-page price.

Why this rubric protects you

Every vendor's marketing benchmark is run on the vendor's favourable conditions.

This rubric removes that advantage: one held-out test set, one harness, one

scoring rubric, applied identically. It is the same discipline the

benchmark-factory enforces internally — and the reason we publish it openly is

that an honest buyer running an honest bake-off is the outcome we are willing to

be measured by.
