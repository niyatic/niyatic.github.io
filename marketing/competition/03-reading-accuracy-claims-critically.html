Reading a document-AI vendor's accuracy claims critically

Every document-AI vendor publishes an accuracy number. Almost none of them

publish enough context to interpret it. This post is a checklist for reading

those claims — applied without favouritism, including to HyperAPI (by Hyperbots).

The seven questions a headline accuracy number cannot answer

When you see "99% accuracy" or "best-in-class extraction," ask:

1. Accuracy of *what field*?

Header fields (vendor, invoice number, total) and line-item rows are different

difficulty classes. A blended number is dominated by whichever class has more

fields. Demand a per-field-type breakdown. If the vendor only has a blended

number, they have not measured the hard part.

2. On *what documents*?

A born-digital PDF with a native text layer is an easy task. A sub-200-DPI skewed

scan or a smartphone photo with glare is a hard one. A number measured on clean

digital documents tells you nothing about your scanned-invoice backlog. Demand

the document-quality stratification.

3. What is the *denominator*?

"99% accuracy" — out of how many documents? n = 50 and n = 50,000 are not the

same evidence. A cell with n < 100 is low-confidence by the benchmark-factory

standard and should not anchor a buying decision.

4. Where is the *confidence interval*?

A point estimate with no interval cannot be compared to another point estimate.

If two vendors quote 98.5% and 98.1% with no CIs, you cannot tell whether that

0.4-point gap is real or noise. Demand a 95% CI — or an honest statement of why

one is not available.

5. Is it accuracy, or is it *accuracy after human review*?

Some vendors quote a number that includes a manual-review correction step. That

is a measure of the *human-in-the-loop process*, not the model. The honest unit

is automated-output accuracy *before* review. The cost-normalization convention

makes this explicit: no manual-review augmentation is assumed for any approach,

including HyperAPI.

6. Is the comparison *apples-to-apples*?

This is the subtle one, and the Hyperbots comparative study confronts it head-on.

A fine-tuned model — one that has seen task-matched training data — compared

against a zero-shot model that has not is not an apples-to-apples comparison.

The fine-tuned model has an information advantage by construction. The honest

study marks fine-tuned rows [FT] everywhere and calls every cross-setting delta

"direction-only." A vendor comparison that mixes fine-tuned and zero-shot rows

without flagging it is misleading even if every individual number is true.

7. Can a stranger *rerun it*?

The benchmark-factory bar: every number is backed by a literal rerunnable

command, on a pinned model revision, pinned dataset snapshot, pinned harness

commit, pinned decoding. A number with no rerun path "does not exist" by that

standard. See post 08 on reproducibility.

How to read the HyperBots numbers critically — including the unflattering parts

Apply the same checklist to our own SAVIOR-Bench numbers, and here is what an

honest read finds:

  • Field breakdown: SAVIOR-Bench reports OCR transcription (recall/precision),

layout (PaIRS), and parse-intent (F1/WER) — separate axes, good. But it does

*not* yet ship a full field-type × document-quality stratified grid; that is a

convention for future ranking-grade runs.

  • n: the OCR table reports n = 509. The parse-intent table reports **no

n** — the attested source does not carry one, and we report it as unknown

rather than inventing one.

  • CI: ci95 = null everywhere. The attested source has only aggregate

scores per model, so no bootstrap CI can be derived. Every delta is

direction-only. We say so.

  • Apples-to-apples: three rows are Hyperbots fine-tunes; every other row is

zero-shot. Marked [FT]. The fine-tune leads OCR recall by +0.0061 and

parse-intent F1 by +0.0276 over the best documented zero-shot anchor — and

*loses* on precision and layout. That is a narrow, mixed result, not a blowout.

  • Reproducibility: the W6 audit ran the factory harness against all 23

parse-intent rows and re-derived 0/23 — for structural reasons (no adapters

for hosted vendor APIs, the in-house corpus is not in the repo, no

per-document score vectors). The numbers stand at the *attested* tier, not the

*reproduced* tier, and we label them that way.

If our own numbers do not survive this checklist cleanly, we say so. That is the

point — the checklist is the asset, not the bravado.

The rule for competitor claims

When you read the LLM-document-parsing API's or any other vendor's accuracy claims, hold them to the

exact same seven questions. Do not accept a headline number from anyone. And note

the inverse: in this blog series, we will not publish a "HyperAPI beats the LLM-document-parsing API by

X%" number unless it passes all seven — sourced, stratified, with n and CI, on

a rerunnable command. Until then it is PENDING MEASUREMENT.
