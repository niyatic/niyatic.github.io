Reducto and the modern document-AI landscape — an honest map

A buyer evaluating document-AI needs a map of the space before a scoreboard. This

post is that map. It is deliberately descriptive, not comparative — it does not

claim any vendor is better than another. Where a capability is stated, it is

either sourced to the vendor's own public docs or flagged as needing

verification.

The six approach categories

The benchmark-factory groups the space into six approach categories. A buyer's

first job is to figure out which category they are actually shopping in, because

the categories have different cost models, different failure modes, and different

integration paths.

Where HyperAPI (by Hyperbots) sits

HyperAPI is a document-AI API by Hyperbots focused on financial documents. The

distinguishing element, per the internal materials, is not a single category

label but an *approach*: small open backbones fine-tuned on financial-document

data (Hyperbots IP, LoRA on Qwen2.5-VL-3B/7B), plus a published, instrumented

benchmark (SAVIOR-Bench) and the benchmark-factory measurement discipline.

The honest positioning, from the comparative study: the fine-tune edge is **real

but narrow** — top-of-panel parse-intent F1 and OCR recall, by small margins, and

*losing* on precision and layout to strong zero-shot systems. The differentiator

a buyer should weigh is the *package*: ownable small models, a large WER

reduction, low serving cost (~$0.19 / 1,000 successful calls, model-serving cost

not product price), and a transparent metric framework — not a raw-magnitude

accuracy blowout.

The honest map — what the categories trade off

  • Hyperscaler doc AI: broad, reliable, per-page priced; can be expensive at

scale and is a fixed-schema-ish black box you cannot fine-tune deeply.

  • Vertical-SaaS extraction: great AP-team UX for one doc type; less flexible

outside it.

  • Frontier LLM-vision: flexible, strong zero-shot, but `schema_mismatch_

hallucination` is a frequent error mode and per-token cost scales with document

size.

  • Legacy enterprise OCR: mature compliance, on-prem; brittle on unseen

layouts.

  • Custom-trainable / fine-tuned approaches (where HyperAPI's fine-tunes sit):

adapt to a corpus distribution; require training data; the edge is real but —

per our own data — narrow, not total.

  • Open-source DIY: lowest direct cost, highest engineering burden; you own

every failure mode.

No category dominates. The right choice depends on document mix, volume, accuracy

tolerance, and whether you need to own the model.

How to use this map

1. Identify your category. If you are feeding documents into a RAG/agent

pipeline, you are likely shopping parsing-API vendors (Reducto, and others).

If you are automating finance-team invoice posting, you may be shopping

vertical-SaaS or finance-tuned APIs.

2. Shortlist within the category — cross-category "comparisons" are usually

apples-to-oranges.

3. Run the buyer's rubric (post 10) on the shortlist, on *your* documents.
