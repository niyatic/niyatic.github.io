The modern document-AI landscape — an honest map

A buyer evaluating document AI needs a map of the space before a scoreboard. This post is that map. It is deliberately descriptive, not comparative — it does not rank vendors. It describes the *categories* of approach, because the categories have different cost models, different failure modes, and different integration paths, and your first job is to figure out which one you're actually shopping in.

This map names no vendors on purpose. Cross-category "X beats Y" comparisons are usually apples-to-oranges; the useful work is matching your document mix and constraints to a *category*, then shortlisting within it.

A modern API-first vendor often spans more than one category — e.g. a parsing API that is LLM-vision under the hood but priced and packaged like vertical SaaS. So "which category" is a question about the *dominant cost + failure model*, not a rigid bucket.

What each category trades off

  • Hyperscaler doc AI — broad, reliable, per-page priced; can get expensive at scale and behaves like a fixed-schema black box you can't fine-tune deeply. Failure mode we've measured on long invoices: table fragmentation — it reads the invoice *total* correctly but sprays the line items (e.g. a 323-line invoice returned as 332 rows at F1 0.009; an 88-row table fragmented to 2,150 rows). Great at the headline number, weak at the rows underneath.
  • Vertical-SaaS extraction — excellent AP-team UX for one doc type; less flexible outside it. The UX *is* the product; the model is a means.
  • Frontier LLM-vision — flexible, strong zero-shot, genuinely good at many documents. Two structural costs: per-token price scales with document size, and the two error modes we see most are schema-mismatch hallucination and a hard output-token truncation cliff on very long tables (a 100+ line-item invoice can stop emitting mid-table). Also: the premium tier is *not* automatically the most accurate — measure per corpus (see the companion D-01 finding).
  • Legacy enterprise OCR — mature compliance, on-prem control; brittle on unseen layouts.
  • Custom-trainable / fine-tuned — adapts to *your* corpus distribution; requires training data; the edge over zero-shot is, in our own honest measurement, real but narrow (small backbone fine-tunes lead on some metrics by small margins and *lose* on others to strong zero-shot systems). The differentiator is the *package* — ownable small models, low serving cost, a transparent measured benchmark — not a raw-magnitude accuracy blowout.
  • Open-source DIY — lowest direct cost, highest engineering burden; you own every failure mode.

No category dominates. The right choice depends on document mix, volume, accuracy tolerance, integration target, and whether you need to *own* the model.

Where an API-native, finance-fine-tuned approach fits

Among these, the approach this blog's authors take is custom-trainable, finance-specialized, and API-native: small open-source backbones fine-tuned by Hyperbots (Hyperbots IP) on financial-document data, exposed as primitive-resolved endpoints (parse / classify / split / match / validate / augment / reason) rather than one end-to-end call, with a published, reproducible benchmark behind it. Honest positioning: the fine-tune edge is real-but-narrow; the bet is the *architecture and the measurement discipline*, not a single magic number. Product accuracy is referenced only "per apis.hyperbots.com"; benchmark figures are model parse/extract performance.

How to use this map

1. Identify your category. Feeding documents into a RAG/agent pipeline → you're shopping parsing-API / LLM-vision vendors. Automating finance-team invoice posting → vertical-SaaS or finance-tuned APIs.

2. Shortlist within the category. Cross-category comparisons mislead.

3. **Run the buyer's rubric on *your* documents** — especially the long tail (invoices past ~100 line items, multi-currency, unusual layouts), because that's where every category's real failure mode lives.
