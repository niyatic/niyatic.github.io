The modern document-AI landscape — an engineering map

A buyer evaluating document AI needs a map of the space before a scoreboard. This post is that map, written for the person who has to integrate and operate the thing. It is deliberately descriptive, not comparative — it does not rank vendors. It describes the *categories* of approach, because the categories have different pipeline architectures, different cost models, different failure mechanisms, and different integration contracts — and your first job is to figure out which one you're actually shopping in.

This map names no vendors on purpose. Cross-category "X beats Y" claims are usually apples-to-oranges; the useful engineering work is matching your document mix and constraints to a *category*, understanding the mechanism by which that category fails on your hard documents, then shortlisting within it. The few proper names that appear below (Azure DI, Gemini, GPT-4o, Textract, PaddleOCR) appear only as measured benchmark baselines, each with a citation — not as product comparisons.

Honesty contract for this post. Every number cites an on-disk artifact. Where a comparative number does not exist yet, the cell reads PENDING MEASUREMENT — it is not filled in. The Hyperbots-side figures are model parse/extract performance, attested SAVIOR-Bench v1 (user-attested 2026-05-18, ci95=null, not factory-reproduced), or factory-reproduced dataset_95 extract runs where so labelled. Product accuracy is referenced only "per apis.hyperbots.com." The fine-tune edge described here is real but narrow; the bet is architecture and measurement discipline, not a magic accuracy number — and we show our losses, not just our wins.

The next section is the part most landscape posts skip: how each category actually moves a document from bytes to records.

2. Per-category architecture — how a document actually gets processed

Every document-AI system is some arrangement of the same stages: image conditioning → text/region recognition (OCR or vision tokens) → layout/reading-order resolution → field & line-item extraction → schema mapping → validation/enrichment. What separates the categories is *which stages are deterministic vs learned, which are fused vs separable, and which are observable vs hidden.*

2.1 Hyperscaler document AI

Pipeline: a server-side OCR/layout stack (detection + recognition + a layout transformer) feeds a prebuilt extractor head trained to a fixed schema (invoice, receipt, ID, etc.). Reading order and table structure are recovered by a dedicated layout model; the extractor maps recognized spans onto schema keys.

  • Deterministic vs learned: OCR and layout are learned; the schema and the key set are fixed by the product, not by you. Custom-template variants let you train a thin extraction head, but the backbone is frozen and managed.
  • Strength: the layout model is genuinely good, so transcription recall is high — on SAVIOR-Bench OCR, the strong cloud baselines Textract (recall 0.9233) and Azure-DI (0.9202) sit near the top of a 21-system panel (SAVIOR-Bench v1 §5.1.2, attested, ci95=null).
  • Failure mechanism (measured): table-structure fragmentation. Because the table extractor is a fixed component that must segment rows from a recovered layout, long/dense tables overwhelm its row-segmentation heuristics. On the ten longest invoices in dataset_95 (49–323 line items), the prebuilt-invoice extractor read the summary total on 9 of 9 docs but landed line-item-set F1 at 0.3439 mean with line-count preservation 0.6396 mean (E-01, factory-reproduced 2026-05-25). On the 323-line 51.json it returned 332 rows — count-close (preservation 0.9729) but F1 0.0094 (1 TP, 109 FP, 101 FN). On an 88-row table it emitted 2,150 rows (preservation 0.041); on a 115-row invoice it returned 0 rows. Great at the headline number, structurally weak at the rows underneath.

2.2 Vertical-SaaS extraction

Pipeline: an extraction model (often itself a hyperscaler or a fine-tuned head) wrapped in a doc-type-specific product — queues, dashboards, exception screens, ERP connectors. The model is a means; the workflow UX is the product.

  • Deterministic vs learned: extraction is learned; the value-add is the deterministic workflow and connectors around it.
  • Strength: excellent for one document type with a known posting flow.
  • Failure mechanism: flexibility falls off a cliff outside the supported doc type, and the model's own failure modes (whatever backbone it uses) are inherited but hidden behind the product surface — you cannot see a per-primitive score, only a product outcome.

2.3 Frontier LLM-vision

Pipeline: the page is encoded to vision tokens; a single autoregressive decoder, conditioned on your prompt, emits the structured output (usually JSON) token by token. OCR, layout reasoning, table-structure recovery, schema mapping, and extraction are fused into one generation under one token budget.

  • Deterministic vs learned: almost nothing is deterministic — the entire pipeline is one learned forward pass. This is the source of both its flexibility and its two structural failure modes.
  • Strength: strong zero-shot. On SAVIOR-Bench OCR, GPT-4o leads precision at 0.9322 (ahead of both fine-tuned rows); Gemini-2.5-Flash posts recall 0.9144 (SAVIOR-Bench v1 §5.1.2, attested).
  • Failure mechanism A — the output-token truncation cliff. Because every line item is emitted as decoded tokens, a long table consumes the output budget. On dataset_95, Gemini hits a hard JSON-truncation cliff at max_output_tokens=32768 on invoices with 100+ line items — it simply stops emitting mid-table (E-01). The token-budget math is in §4.
  • Failure mechanism B — the premium tier is not automatically the accurate tier. On the dataset_95 extract task, Gemini 2.5 Flash 0.7132 beat Gemini 2.5 Pro 0.6195 micro-F1 (+0.0937) at roughly one-third the per-doc cost ($0.010 vs $0.033) — Pro attempted 93/95 docs vs Flash's 90/95, but extracted *worse* records on the ones it completed (D-01, factory-reproduced 2026-05-25). Capability tier does not predict extraction accuracy on a finance corpus; you have to measure per corpus.

2.4 Legacy enterprise OCR

Pipeline: a mature OCR engine plus template/rule-based extraction, on hardware you operate. Reading order and zones are often template-bound — you draw the regions, the engine fills them.

  • Deterministic vs learned: OCR is learned (older-generation); extraction is largely deterministic templates/rules. Highly auditable, fully on-prem.
  • Strength: compliance posture, data residency, no per-call cloud meter.
  • Failure mechanism: layout drift. Template/zone binding is brittle to any layout it was not authored for — a new vendor template, a shifted logo, a rotated scan moves text out of its expected zone and the extraction misfires. Robustness across layout families is exactly the axis (framework dimension D3) where this category is weakest.

2.5 Custom-trainable / fine-tuned document AI

Pipeline: a VLM or OCR backbone adapted to your corpus distribution (often via LoRA on a small open backbone), then served — and, in the API-native instantiation, decomposed into separately-callable primitives rather than one end-to-end call. See §3 for the decomposition and §5 for the fine-tuning mechanics.

  • Deterministic vs learned: the per-primitive models are learned; the orchestration between primitives (split boundaries, validation rules) can be made deterministic and auditable.
  • Strength: adapts to *your* fields and layouts; the weights are ownable and cheap to serve (§6).
  • Honest failure/limit: the fine-tune edge is real but narrow (§5). Our own current end-to-end dataset_95 micro-F1 is 0.3573 — below all three Gemini tiers (D-01/E-01, factory-reproduced) — shown, not hidden. The bet is the architecture, not a headline number.

2.6 Open-source DIY

Pipeline: you assemble an OCR engine (e.g. PaddleOCR-class) + a layout model + your own extraction/validation code. Every stage is yours.

  • Deterministic vs learned: your choice at every stage — maximum control, maximum engineering burden.
  • Strength: lowest direct cost, full ownership.
  • Failure mechanism: you own *every* failure mode in this document — truncation, fragmentation, layout drift, schema mismatch — and you own the eval harness that would even detect them.

No category dominates. The right choice depends on document mix, volume, accuracy tolerance, integration target, and whether you need to *own* the model. The rest of this post is the engineering substance behind that judgment.

2.7 The same document through each category — a worked trace

Take one hard input: a 323-line-item supplier invoice, two-column header (bill-to / ship-to), table spanning three pages, totals on the last page. Watch where each architecture commits its error.

  • Hyperscaler doc AI. OCR + layout recover the page well; the prebuilt extractor reads the header and the summary total correctly, then its row-segmentation heuristic mis-groups the dense table — emitting 332 rows at line-item F1 0.0094 (E-01 51.json). The error is *localized in table-structure recovery* and is invisible if you only check the total.
  • Frontier LLM-vision. Vision tokens + one decoder begin emitting JSON; header fields land, the line-item array streams out, and at ~12k–24k output tokens the decoder hits the 32,768 ceiling and stops mid-array (E-01 truncation; §4.1). The record looks valid up to the truncation point. The error is *in the fusion of a long table with a fixed output budget.*
  • Legacy enterprise OCR. If the vendor template was authored for this layout, it extracts cleanly; if the two-column header or a shifted total moved text out of an authored zone, it drifts and mis-fills. The error is *template brittleness to unseen layout.*
  • Custom-trainable / primitive-resolved. split chunks the three-page table at page boundaries *before* extraction, so no single parse call must emit 332 rows under one ceiling; validate checks line-count preservation and trips an auditable boundary if a chunk truncates. The long-invoice case becomes *handled*, not a silent cliff (E-01 §"Why API-native…"). The honest caveat: the current end-to-end dataset_95 baseline is still 0.3573 (§2.5) — the architecture is the path, not a finished win.

Same document, four *different* failure locations. That is why "which category" is really "which failure mechanism can you live with, and can you see it when it fires."

3. The seven-primitive decomposition — and why it beats end-to-end on hard documents

The single most consequential architectural decision is whether the system is one end-to-end call or a pipeline of separable primitives. The framework this blog's authors use decomposes document processing into seven primitives (the 7-primitive × 2-process O2C/P2P frame; FRAMEWORK-2026-05-25 §2.2):

PrimitiveWhat it does mechanicallyCanonical metric family
parseraw bytes → structured fields + line items + table cellsfield-P/R/F1, cell-P/R, TEDS, line-item count accuracy
classifyassign each input to a doc-type labelmacro-F1, AUROC, per-class P/R
splitcut a multi-doc bundle at correct page boundariesboundary-F1, page-attribution accuracy
matchtie a doc to others (2/3/N-way) by IDs, line items, totalsper-leg P/R, end-to-end match rate, dollar coverage
validatecheck extracted/matched data against business rulesrule-coverage F1, false-block rate
augmentenrich via master-data / tax / FX / catalogue lookupsenrichment hit-rate, lookup precision, freshness
reasondecide exceptions (approve / hold / route) with a tracedecision agreement, hallucination rate, traceability

Why decomposition is the structural fix for long/complex documents

The reason a 300-row invoice breaks an end-to-end extractor is that extract is being asked to do split, layout reasoning, and table-structure recovery all in one shot, inside one token budget. Both measured cliffs of §2 (truncation, fragmentation) are *consequences of fusion*. Decompose the work and the cliff moves:

  • split as a first-class primitive. A 323-row invoice is chunked at page/section boundaries *before* extraction, so no single generation has to emit 332 rows under a 32k-token ceiling. The truncation cliff (§4) becomes a handled case instead of a silent stop (E-01).
  • parse → validate as separate, measurable steps. Line-count preservation becomes an *auditable boundary* (validate) rather than a silent truncation buried inside one JSON blob. A truncated table now trips a rule instead of returning a plausible-but-short record.
  • Per-primitive serving. Each primitive is a separately-measured, separately-improvable model. That is *how* a number like 0.3573 can move — you can find which primitive is the bottleneck instead of re-prompting a monolith and hoping.

The end-to-end argument ("one model does everything, simpler integration") is real for short, clean documents. It inverts on the long tail — exactly the documents (100+ line items, multi-page tables, multi-currency) where the money and the audit risk live. Decomposition trades a little integration simplicity for observability and a movable failure boundary.

3.1 Why this maps onto a finance process, not just a parse call

The primitives are not an arbitrary slicing — they trace the two finance processes the documents live in (FRAMEWORK-2026-05-25 §2.1):

  • P2P (procure-to-pay): PO → goods-receipt → supplier invoice → 3-way match → validate → GL post. The money is in match (invoice ↔ PO ↔ GRN) and validate (tax, duplicate, totals) — stages a single end-to-end "extraction" call structurally *cannot own*, because they are corpus-against-corpus operations, not single-document generations.
  • O2C (order-to-cash): sales order → invoice → remittance → cash application. Here augment (master-data, FX) and reason (dispute routing) carry the load.

An end-to-end black box can do parse. It cannot do match against a PO corpus it has never seen, and it cannot expose a validate rule-coverage number. The decomposition exists because the finance value is in the primitives an end-to-end call collapses away.

4. Failure-mode mechanisms — root causes, not just symptoms

4.1 The output-token truncation cliff (the token-budget math)

An LLM-vision system emits structured output as decoded tokens against a fixed max_output_tokens ceiling. A line-item row in JSON is not free: a single row like

{"description":"...", "qty":3, "unit_price":124.00, "line_total":372.00},

costs on the order of tens of output tokens once keys, punctuation, and field values are tokenized. At ~40–80 output tokens/row (description length dominates), a 100-row table already consumes ~4k–8k output tokens; a 300-row table consumes ~12k–24k *before* the header fields, and dense descriptions push it past the 32,768-token ceiling Gemini enforces. The decoder stops mid-array. The JSON is syntactically truncated, the trailing rows simply do not exist — and unless you parse for completeness, the failure is silent. This is the measured cliff on dataset_95's 100+ line-item invoices (42.json at 115 rows, 51.json at 323 rows both truncate on Flash and Pro; E-01). Root cause: table length is unbounded but the output budget is fixed, and the two are fused in one generation.

4.2 Table-structure recovery / fragmentation

A fixed-schema extractor recovers rows by segmenting a layout into cells, then grouping cells into rows. On long/dense tables the row-grouping heuristic mis-segments: one logical row splits into two (a wrapped description becomes a phantom row), or two tight rows merge (qty of row 1 binds to price of row 2 and the arithmetic stops reconciling). The result is the fragmentation measured in E-01: an 88-row table returned as 2,150 rows (preservation 0.041), a 323-row table returned as 332 rows at F1 0.0094. Root cause: row segmentation is a heuristic over recovered layout, and its error rate grows with row density and count — the opposite of the easy-80%-of-documents regime most accuracy claims are made on.

4.3 Schema-mismatch hallucination

When asked for a fixed schema the source does not contain (a field absent on this vendor's template), a generative model fills it anyway — inventing a plausible value. This is the schema_mismatch_hallucination bucket of the closed 7-bucket error taxonomy (blog 01). It is a *precision* failure: the model emitted a field that should not exist. Root cause: a generative decoder is trained to continue, not to abstain — absent an explicit "not present" path and a validation gate, the path of least resistance is a confident wrong value.

4.4 Layout drift

Text on a page means something *because of where it sits* — the number to the right of "Total Due" is the total because of position, not glyphs. A reading-order-naive system linearizes a two-column header into "Acme / Globex / 123 Main St / 456 Oak Ave…", shuffling bill-to and ship-to: characters all correct, fields all wrong (layout_misread / reading_order_error; blog 02). Template-bound legacy OCR drifts the same way when a layout moves outside its authored zones. Root cause: transcription accuracy and extraction accuracy are different things, and the gap between them is layout — which is why a serious benchmark scores layout *separately* (PaIRS; §5/§6) instead of folding it into one accuracy number.

The unifying insight. Three of these four mechanisms (truncation, fragmentation, schema hallucination) are amplified by *fusing many sub-tasks into one generation under one budget*. That is the engineering case for §3's decomposition: separable primitives put a measurable, recoverable boundary between the sub-tasks that a monolith silently runs together.

5. Metrics taxonomy — why one blended "accuracy %" misleads

"Accuracy" on a document is not one quantity. A document has a vendor name, an invoice number, a total, a tax line, a date, a dozen line items, and payment terms — each a different recognition problem. A model can nail the headers and mangle every row, and a blended "98%" hides that completely. Here is what each metric actually captures:

MetricCapturesNotable property
Field-level micro-F1header/field extraction quality, harmonic mean of P/Rthe honest single number *per field type*, never blended page-wide
Line-count preservationdid the system emit the right *number* of rowscatches truncation/fragmentation independent of content
Line-item-set F1 / precision / recallrow-level correctness (a row is TP only if all cells correct *and* bound to the right logical row)recall catches merges/drops; precision catches splits/phantom rows
PaIRSpairwise spatial-relationship fidelity (layout/reading-order)PC-only: directly comparable only among prompt-controlled systems; a format-compatibility proxy otherwise (SAVIOR-Bench §2.2)
WER (word error rate)raw transcription error, ↓ betterexposes verbose hallucination: F1 can survive while WER explodes
OCR normalized edit distancecharacter-level transcription fidelity, ↓ betterchar-level rewards a 1-char error that still breaks a key match — why word-level metrics matter for downstream extraction

The measured proof that a single number lies

On E-01's long-invoice subset the prebuilt extractor matched the summary total on 9 of 9 documents while landing line-item-set F1 at 0.3439 mean. A buyer told only "matches the total" would conclude the tool works; a buyer who also sees the 0.34 line-item F1 knows it is useless for the GL. The same tool is excellent and useless depending on which metric you read — which is the entire argument against a blended accuracy headline.

Two more decoupling examples from SAVIOR-Bench (attested, ci95=null):

  • Recall and layout decouple. PaddleOCR-VL-1.5 is near the bottom on recall (0.7220, rank 17) yet first on PaIRS (0.8766); Textract is second on recall (0.9233) but mid-pack on PaIRS (0.5117). High transcription recall does not imply faithful reading order.
  • WER and F1 are not monotone. PaddleOCR-VL-1.5 reaches parse-intent F1 0.7260 at WER 2.9641; DeepSeek-OCR-2 reaches WER 12.3541 at F1 0.3430. A system can hallucinate verbose output, score moderate field-F1, and have an unreliable raw transcription — visible *only* because both metrics are reported.

The closed error-mode taxonomy (what "wrong" actually means)

A wrong extraction is wrong in one of seven specific ways; bucketing the failures tells you what to fix and what each failure *costs* (blog 01):

BucketWhat happenedHits which metric

The cost asymmetry matters operationally: a refusal_no_output routes to review (cheap); a numeric_format_error that posts a wrong total to the ledger uncaught is the expensive one. A model's *error-mode mix*, not just its headline accuracy, sets its true cost (§7).

Demand from any vendor (including this one): field-level P/R/F1 by field type; a document-quality stratification; row-level F1 for tables (not cell-level); n and a 95% CI on every cell — or an honest ci95=null with the reason; the error-mode breakdown; and a rerunnable command behind every number.

6. Fine-tuning mechanics — the small+ownable vs large+rented argument

The custom-trainable category's technical claim is that a small open VLM backbone, LoRA-fine-tuned on your domain, and served per-primitive beats *for your purposes* a large zero-shot model you rent by the token. Here is the actual recipe and the honest result.

The recipe (SAVIOR-Bench Appendix A)

  • Method: LoRA, rank 32, α 64, applied to the vision-encoder final layer + the language decoder.
  • Backbones: Qwen2.5-VL-3B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct (open-source backbones fine-tuned by Hyperbots → "Hyperbots IP").
  • Learning rates: 1e-4 (vision) / 5e-4 (language); batch size 1, gradient accumulation 10; 7 epochs (3B) / 5 epochs (7B).
  • Hardware: 2× H100 80 GB, CUDA 12.3. Inference: top-p 0.9, temperature 0.1.

The precision-vs-recall-vs-layout tradeoff (shown with our losses)

Fine-tuning is not a uniform lift. On SAVIOR-Bench v1 (attested 2026-05-18, ci95=null, [FT] = fine-tuned, not apples-to-apples vs zero-shot rows):

The engineering case anyway

The case for small+fine-tuned+ownable does not rest on a magnitude blowout — it rests on three properties:

  • Ownable weights. A LoRA adapter on an open backbone is yours to version, audit, and serve on your own iron — no managed-service drift, no API-version freeze risk under your benchmark.
  • Serving economics. A 3B/7B backbone is cheap to serve relative to a frontier-scale rented model — see §7. The infra-derived figure is ~$0.19 per 1,000 successful calls (model-serving cost, not a product price).
  • Per-primitive improvability. Because the chain is decomposed (§3), each primitive's model is measured and improved independently — which is the mechanism by which our honest 0.3573 dataset_95 baseline is a number that *moves*, not a wall.

The residual is honest too: the multilingual + very-dense-layout cluster persists even after fine-tuning (SAVIOR-Bench §5.6) — fine-tuning narrows it, it does not eliminate it.

Serving profile of the fine-tuned path

The small-backbone serving profile is the other half of the economic argument. On the SAVIOR-Train inference path (attested, SAVIOR-Bench §5.4/§5.5):

  • Latency: p50 2,548 ms, p99 3,319 ms.
  • Failure rate: 3% — KV-cache-bound; a 2-GPU path is the noted mitigation. (This is a serving-failure rate, distinct from extraction accuracy.)
  • Cost: $0.000186 per attempted call, $0.000191 per successful call ≈ $0.19 per 1,000 successful calls, on an A100 40 GB at ~$2/hr.

These are infrastructure-derived model-serving figures (not a product price, not factory-reproduced beyond the attested source); the point is the *shape* — a 3B/7B backbone serves at sub-cent-per-thousand-call economics, which a frontier rented model cannot match at the same ownership posture.

7. Cost-model economics — the technical view

The pricing *unit* is an architectural fact, not a billing detail, because it determines how cost scales with your document mix and with failure.

Pricing unitScales withHidden risk
Per-token (LLM-vision)document *size* — input vision tokens + output JSON tokenstruncation-retry amplifies it: a doc that hits the cliff (§4.1) is re-run at full cost, often more than once, and a dense 3-page statement can cost several times a sparse 1-page invoice on the identical pipeline
Per-page (hyperscaler)page count onlypredictable, but a fragmented long table (§4.2) bills as a "success" while the rows are wrong — you pay full price for an unusable record
Per-block / per-run (some vertical SaaS)number of processing blocks per doccost varies *per document* with block count — hard to forecast at month-end
Per-credit / mode-tieredwhich mode you invoke per pagea premium/multimodal mode costs notably more than a fast tier; easy to under-budget
Per-correct-extraction (the honest view)spend ÷ extractions that *passed scoring*none — this is the figure that penalizes silent failure; it is what a buyer should actually compute

Why per-correct is the only honest cost view

A 2-cent/page API at 90% success has a per-correct cost of ~2.2 cents; a 3-cent/page API at 99% has ~3.03 cents — and the gap narrows or *inverts* once you price the failures (a wrong total posted to the ledger costs a correction cycle and audit exposure, not just the failed call). The most expensive failure is the silent one — a model that truncates a long invoice and returns a plausible short record bills as a success and corrupts the GL downstream. Cost and accuracy are one decision, not two. Show three figures together — per-page, per-call, per-correct — and compute per-correct on *your* document size distribution, never on an "average" document. (Cost-normalization convention; blog 09.)

8. Integration architecture — observability is an operational requirement, not a nicety

There are two integration contracts on offer:

  • End-to-end black box. One call: PDF in, JSON (or markdown chunks) out. Simple to wire; opaque to operate. When accuracy degrades you cannot tell *which* sub-task regressed — OCR, layout, table recovery, or schema mapping — because they are fused. Your only lever is re-prompting the monolith.
  • API-native, primitive-resolved endpoints. parse / classify / split / match / validate / augment / reason are each separately addressable, each with its own metric and its own line in the bill (§3, §7). When something regresses, per-primitive observability tells you it was split boundary-F1 or validate false-block rate — and you fix that primitive without disturbing the rest.

Per-primitive measurability is an operational requirement because the failures that matter (§4) live in specific sub-tasks: truncation is a split/parse boundary problem, fragmentation is a parse table-fidelity problem, schema hallucination is a validate problem. A black box that fails on a 300-row invoice gives you a worse number and no diagnosis. A primitive-resolved chain gives you the same bad number *with the failing stage named* — which is the difference between a problem you can move and a wall.

This is where the custom-trainable, finance-specialized, API-native approach this blog's authors take fits the map: small open-source backbones fine-tuned by Hyperbots (Hyperbots IP), exposed as primitive-resolved endpoints with per-primitive observability, behind a published reproducible benchmark. Honest positioning: the fine-tune edge is real-but-narrow (§6); the differentiator is the *architecture and the measurement discipline* — primitives you can measure, weights you can own, and a benchmark that shows the losses. Product accuracy is referenced only "per apis.hyperbots.com"; the benchmark figures above are model parse/extract performance.

8.1 Reproducibility is part of the architecture, not a footnote

The same observability discipline applies to the *evidence*. A benchmark number you cannot rerun is a marketing asset, not a measurement (blog 08). A trustworthy cell needs: model pinning (ID + revision/sha, no floating latest), dataset pinning (name + snapshot sha256 + frozen split), harness pinning (git commit + PINS.txt with Python + every dep + seed), decoding pinning (temperature/top-p/max-tokens fixed per task family), and a literal rerunnable command stored with the cell.

The honest part — and it is itself a credibility signal — is that SAVIOR-Bench v1 is attested, not factory-reproduced. When the pinned harness was run against all 23 parse-intent rows (W6 audit), 0/23 re-derived green for three structural reasons: the offline SEED harness has no adapters for hosted-API vendor models; the in-house parse-intent corpus is not in the repo; and the attested source carries only aggregate F1/WER, so a bootstrap CI cannot be computed without fabricating per-document scores — which the team refused to do. Nothing was silently flipped or retracted. That is the difference between an honest benchmark and a marketing one: not that it has no gaps, but that it names them, keeps ci95=null rather than inventing intervals, and tells you which evidence tier each number sits on.

9. How to use this map

  • Identify your category by dominant pipeline + cost unit + failure mode, not by marketing label. Feeding documents into a RAG/agent pipeline → you're shopping parsing-API / LLM-vision. Automating finance-team posting with P2P/O2C matching → vertical-SaaS or a finance-tuned primitive API.
  • Probe the failure mechanism for your hard documents. Ask for numbers on invoices past 100 line items, not the blended average — the truncation and fragmentation cliffs live there. Ask whether the system can emit a split/validate boundary or only one fused generation.
  • Separate the metrics. Distinguish summary-total accuracy (a tool can match the total 9/9) from line-item F1 (0.34) — they measure different things. Demand row-level F1, a quality stratification, and n + CI (or an honest ci95=null).
  • Compute per-correct cost on your own document-size distribution, and price the silent-failure mode explicitly.
  • Run the rubric on your own corpus. A model's general-leaderboard rank does not predict its accuracy on your invoice mix — Flash beating Pro is the proof (D-01).
