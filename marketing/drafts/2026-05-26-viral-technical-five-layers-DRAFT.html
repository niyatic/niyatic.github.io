Five layers of production document AI — what doesn't show up in a one-number leaderboard

A vendor demo shows you one screen and one number. Production document AI for finance, in our experience, is five layers — and the gap between the demo and the deploy is where the real engineering lives.

Layer 1 — Open-source backbones, fine-tuned, primitive-resolved.

Our public benchmark — SAVIOR-Bench v1 — gives the headline numbers: parse-intent +0.0276 F1 over the best zero-shot system, OCR +0.0061 recall. Real, but narrow. The model is open-source backbones fine-tuned by Hyperbots (Hyperbots IP). Every claim in the paper is attested or audited honestly — and the W6 audit re-derived 0 of 23 SAVIOR cells. We published that. That's the kind of negative finding most companies bury.

Layer 2 — Multi-engine architecture, mode-routed.

Production doesn't run one OCR engine. It runs several, composed under per-document mode routing — because invoice OCR, receipt parsing, bank-statement extraction, and shipping-document parsing are different problems that the same engine plays differently on. The composition pattern is documented internally; the routing logic is part of the IP.

Layer 3 — GPU-served inference, region-mobile.

The infrastructure is a GPU pool you can migrate (we recently moved one of ours across regions without dropping production traffic). Latency, throughput, and cold-start aren't marketing numbers — they're per-primitive infrastructure metrics, instrumented at the call site, watched on a deploy dashboard, and budgeted per request.

Layer 4 — Observability per primitive call.

Documents → primitives (parse, classify, split, match, validate, augment, reason) → results. Each step is a measurable boundary. Without per-primitive observability, the "accuracy" of an end-to-end pipeline is unfalsifiable; it becomes whatever the demo says it is. We instrument every layer because the customer is going to ask "where did it break?" and "I don't know" is not an answer.

Layer 5 — Documentation that tracks the deploy.

The architecture, the flows, the schema, the integration requirements, the observability plan — all kept in sync with what's actually running, audited against the live system, and reviewed before each release. "We have docs" is not the same as "the docs match production." Most don't. Ours do, because the credibility of the public surface depends on the rigor of the internal one.

What's on the public surface:

SAVIOR-Bench v1 · the SEED leaderboard (12 cells, reproducible on a laptop) · the W6 reproducibility audit · the published $0.05/page pricing (volume sized on a call).

What stays internal:

The routing, the tenant-integration patterns, the failure-mode taxonomy, the per-primitive instrumentation schema. Both layers exist because one earns the other.

The take I'd argue for.

Most "AI-powered" finance-doc pitches promise an outcome and skip the architecture. The architecture is the moat — not the model, not the prompt, not the pitch. A 99% number on someone's demo doesn't survive contact with the second invoice of the day; a five-layer stack with per-primitive observability does.

The question I'd love to be challenged on.

If you've built or evaluated document AI in production, where did your stack break first — the OCR engine, the routing, the schema, or the observability?
