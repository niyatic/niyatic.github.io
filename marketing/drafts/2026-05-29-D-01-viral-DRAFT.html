We paid 3× for the premium model. It lost.

We benchmarked Gemini 2.5 Pro against Gemini 2.5 Flash on 95 finance invoices — structured line-item extraction, factory-reproduced, scored on micro-F1.

The premium model lost.

  • Gemini 2.5 Flash: 0.7132 F1 · $0.010/doc
  • Gemini 2.5 Pro: 0.6195 F1 · $0.033/doc

Flash beat Pro by +0.0937 F1 — at one-third the cost. The cheaper model was both more accurate *and* cheaper on this corpus.

The obvious objection: did Pro just attempt harder documents? Partly — Pro completed 93 of 95 vs Flash's 90. But on the documents each actually finished, Flash extracted cleaner records on both F1 (0.7132 vs 0.6195) and exact line-count accuracy (0.7333 vs 0.6452). It wasn't a technicality. The cheaper tier was simply better here.

This isn't a "Gemini is bad" post — both are strong models. And it's not a "we win" post: for the record, our own pipeline lands at 0.3573 on this same corpus, below all three Gemini tiers. We publish that because the point doesn't depend on us looking good.

The point is this: the premium tier is a hypothesis, not a fact. On finance documents specifically, the assumption that flagship = most accurate broke — and it cost 3× per document to find out the expensive way.

Two things I'd take into any model-selection decision:

1. Separate attempt-rate from accuracy. A model that returns *something* on 100% of inputs can still be the wrong pick if the something is wrong. Pro attempted more, extracted worse.

2. Measure per-corpus, not per-leaderboard. A model's general ranking does not predict its accuracy on *your* invoice mix. The only number that matters is the one you run on your own documents.

If you're choosing a model for document extraction: run the cheap tier head-to-head before you default to the flagship. The budget line is real.
