Azure DI fragments. Gemini truncates. We published the doc that breaks all three.

We took the ten longest invoices in a finance benchmark — up to 323 line items — and ran them through the two leading cloud prebuilt extractors. Both broke. They broke differently. And the boundary that breaks them also breaks ours.

Azure DI prebuilt-invoice fragments. On a 323-line-item invoice it returned 332 rows — close on the count, F1 0.0094. One true positive, 109 false positives. On another it returned zero rows. On a third it sprayed an 88-row table into 2,150 rows. It nails the invoice *total* 9 times out of 9 — and gets the *line items* wrong. A tool can be right about the number a CFO signs and useless for the GL underneath it.

Gemini 2.5 truncates. Flash lands at 0.7132 micro-F1; Pro at 0.6195 — yes, the premium model loses, at 3× the cost. Both hit a hard JSON-truncation cliff at 32k output tokens on invoices past ~100 line items. The model just stops emitting before the table is done.

Same documents. Two different cliffs. One dataset boundary.

Here's the line most vendor posts would delete: our own pipeline doesn't win this corpus either. Today the Hyperbots IDP chain lands at 0.3573 on it — below Gemini Flash. We published that, because the credibility of everything else we say depends on it.

So this isn't a "we win" post. It's a "here's the measured frontier" post. And the reason the frontier exists is architectural: a 300-row invoice breaks an end-to-end extractor because one model call is doing splitting, layout reasoning, and table recovery all at once, under one token budget. Decompose it — split before parse, validate as an auditable step, open-source backbones fine-tuned per primitive (Hyperbots IP) — and the cliff becomes a handled case instead of a silent failure.

The bet isn't a better single model. It's a pipeline where the long invoice is a case you designed for.

If you evaluate document AI: ask for the numbers on invoices past 100 line items, not the blended average. That's where the truth is.

Where did *your* extractor break first — the long tables, the totals, or the line items underneath?
