An LLM-only parsing API solves one primitive of seven — and that's the honest read

The fastest-growing band in doc-AI is the LLM-only document-parsing API. Send a PDF, get tables and text back, feed it to RAG, ship by Tuesday. Real product shape. Good at what it does.

It's also one primitive of seven.

A finance doc-AI stack does seven jobs of work — parse, classify, split, match, validate, augment, reason. Turning mixed PDFs into approved GL postings with an audit trail needs all of them. A single-call parsing API is built for the first one. That's not a knock — it's a category description.

Where the LLM-only parsing band actually wins.

Out-of-domain generality. A frontier-VLM-backed API handles research papers, contracts, scientific PDFs — classes a finance-tuned model has never seen. On our own SAVIOR-Bench v1 panel, *zero-shot* systems beat the fine-tuned ones on precision and layout. The general-purpose models are not a punching bag.

Where the band stops.

  • split — cutting a 50-page bundle into sub-documents. An LLM sees page tokens; no native sense of boundary.
  • match — three-way match against a corpus of POs and GRNs. That's a database operation. A parsing API has nowhere to put the corpus.
  • validate — business-rule audit. The rule becomes a string the model decides whether to honour — not a rule catalogue a controller can audit.
  • reason — exception decisions with a field-citing justification trace. Different product shape entirely.

Where the gap is small — and where it's wide.

On parse itself, the gap is small. Our fine-tune edge is +0.0276 F1 on parse-intent, +0.0061 recall on OCR. Real but narrow. We published that — the negative finding is the asset. The wide gap isn't at the model. It's at the architecture: the LLM-only band exposes one endpoint where a finance-tuned, primitive-resolved IDP exposes seven.

The buyer-rubric question that actually matters.

Not "whose parse accuracy is higher?" — it's "how many of the seven primitives are first-class named endpoints, with a documented schema, a published metric, and a price line?" Count them on the docs page. Five minutes.

For reference on our side: open-source backbones fine-tuned by Hyperbots (Hyperbots IP), seven primitives exposed, published $0.05/page (volume sized on a call), SEED leaderboard reproducible on a laptop, W6 audit that re-derived 0 of 23 cells under our own factory harness — published, because the finding is the point.

A question.

If you've evaluated an LLM-only parsing API for a finance workflow, where did you hit the seam — split on bundle PDFs, match against your ERP, validate against your rules, or somewhere else?
