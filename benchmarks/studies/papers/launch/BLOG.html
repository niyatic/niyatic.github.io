The fine-tune edge was smaller than we expected — and that's the useful finding

A technical walkthrough of SAVIOR-Bench v1: 509 expert-annotated financial documents, 45 OCR/VLM systems, one frozen prompt, and a deliberately modest result.

TL;DR

We build document-AI for finance. We wanted an honest answer to: how much does fine-tuning an open vision-language model on financial documents actually buy you over the best zero-shot system? So we built a benchmark, ran ~45 systems through it, and measured.

The answer: +0.0061 OCR recall and +0.0276 parse-intent F1 over the best well-documented zero-shot system (+0.0226 against a marginally-higher but under-documented panel row — see below). On OCR precision and layout fidelity, zero-shot systems won. Fine-tuning bought a narrow, metric-specific edge — not a universal one.

We're publishing the small number because it's the true one, and because it's more useful than "our model wins": it tells you exactly where fine-tuning helps and where it doesn't. And it explains why we still ship an API-native pipeline instead of telling people to go fine-tune their own model.

Why we built SAVIOR-Bench

Generic OCR benchmarks under-measure two things that matter for financial documents:

  1. Whole-token fidelity. Downstream LLM extraction needs Total, not

Totl. A one-character edit that char-level CER barely penalizes still breaks a downstream key match. So we score word-level multiset recall and precision: Totl ≠ Total, full penalty, not partial.

  1. Layout fidelity. Reading order and spatial structure determine

whether a table parses at all. A model can recover every token and still emit them in an order that makes the table un-parseable. So we score a pairwise spatial-relationship metric (PaIRS) separately from transcription.

SAVIOR-Bench v1 is 509 expert-annotated financial documents across five corpora — UCSF Industry Documents (46.95%), in-house synthetic (17.88%), misc. public financial (17.49%), Inv3D (9.04%), FUNSD (8.64%) — covering invoices, packing slips, forms, multilingual scans, low-quality PDFs, and complex tables. Inter-annotator agreement is 0.761 (fuzzy string similarity over 187 double-annotated pairs).

We evaluate two task families on identical inputs, one frozen prompt per family, and pinned decoding (temperature 0.1, top-p 0.9, max_tokens 2048, seed 20260516):

  • OCR transcription + layout — word-level recall/precision + PaIRS.
  • Parse-intent extraction — F1 (↑) and word error rate (↓).

The panel: ~21 OCR systems and 23 parse-intent systems spanning leading cloud document-AI APIs, frontier multimodal LLMs, and open-source OCR/VLM stacks. The named systems live in the benchmark paper (naming measured systems is a scientific-benchmark norm and is scoped to that artifact); this blog uses category labels.

The honest disclosure, first

Three rows are fine-tuned: two OCR backbones and the parse-intent system are LoRA fine-tunes (rank 32, α 64) of open Qwen2.5-VL-Instruct backbones on our training split — Hyperbots-IP, open-source backbones fine-tuned by us. Every other row is zero-shot in the vendor's native settings. These are not apples-to-apples comparisons; they are tagged [FT] everywhere they appear in the paper, and the disclosure is the first block of the abstract — not an appendix.

We lead with this because the entire value of the result depends on reading it correctly.

The result

OCR transcription (recall ↑)

The fine-tuned model leads OCR recall at 0.9294. The best zero-shot system reaches 0.9233 — a gap of +0.0061. The next zero-shot system is at 0.9202. Three systems within ~0.01 recall of a fine-tuned model that was specifically trained on this document distribution.

Parse-intent (F1 ↑ / WER ↓)

The fine-tuned parse-intent system leads at F1 0.8328 / WER 0.2589. We anchor the gap on the best well-documented zero-shot system (0.8052 / WER 0.4232): a +0.0276 F1 edge with a large WER reduction (0.2589 vs 0.4232). One panel row posts a marginally higher 0.8102 but is under-documented (no recorded vendor / checkpoint / config); against it the gap is the more conservative +0.0226 F1 — reported as-attested, not used as the anchor. Named systems live in the linked paper. Either way this is where fine-tuning pays off most: structured intent extraction, not raw transcription.

Where fine-tuning loses

This is the part a marketing post would crop. We won't.

  • OCR precision: the best system is zero-shot at 0.9322, *ahead of

both fine-tuned rows* (0.9157, 0.9101).

  • Layout (PaIRS): the leaders are zero-shot (0.8766, 0.8191); the

fine-tuned models (0.7066, 0.7068) do not lead layout at all.

Fine-tuning on SAVIOR-Train improves recall and parse-F1. It does not improve precision or reading-order fidelity. That's a candid counter-result, and it's in the paper's conclusion, not hidden.

Two findings we didn't expect

1. Recall and layout decouple — hard. One open-source system is near the bottom on recall (rank 17 of 21) yet first on layout fidelity. A leading cloud API is second on recall but mid-pack on layout. High transcription recall does not imply faithful reading order. This is the entire reason we score both metrics separately rather than collapsing them — a single OCR number would have hidden this.

2. Verbose hallucination wrecks WER while F1 survives. On parse-intent, a system can hold a moderate field-F1 (0.7260) while its transcription WER explodes to 2.96 — or reach WER 12.35 at F1 0.34. Systems that pad output with hallucinated tokens can look acceptable on structured F1 while their raw transcription is unreliable. Reporting WER alongside F1 exposes this; reporting either alone hides it.

What this is not (said plainly, not footnoted)

A benchmark's credibility is its disclosure discipline. Ours:

  • Attested, not yet factory-reproduced. Every cell is in-house-run and

user-attested (2026-05-18). The deterministic factory harness, pinned environment, and repro-check exist and are in place — but they have not yet been run against these cells. Factory re-reproduction is the next hardening step, and we say exactly that in the paper's reproduction-status box. No cell is asserted as factory-verified.

  • No confidence intervals. ci95=null. We report "no CI" rather than

fabricate an interval. CIs require per-document scores we don't yet emit.

  • No per-class slices. The attested source is corpus-level aggregate.

We do not report a by-class/by-quality/by-field table because generating one would mean fabricating splits the data doesn't contain. We mark that gap explicitly.

  • PaIRS is prompt-controlled-only. Layout fidelity is directly

comparable only among prompt-controllable models; for fixed-schema APIs it's a format-compatibility proxy, labelled as such.

The paper includes a related-work matrix that marks every dimension we do not yet satisfy with an ✗ — no per-category slices, no degradation tracks, no confidence intervals, not yet a regenerable corpus, not yet factory-reproduced. We named the gaps instead of papering over them, and the paper's §10 is the explicit roadmap to close them.

So why do we still ship an API?

If the fine-tune edge over the best zero-shot model is only +0.0061 recall, why not just tell people to use a strong zero-shot system, or fine-tune their own?

Because the leaderboard delta is not where the production value is:

  • Latency: p50 2,548 ms / p99 3,319 ms on the fine-tuned inference path.
  • Cost: ≈ $0.19 per 1,000 successful calls (A100 40 GB at ~$2/hr;

infrastructure-derived, not a price quote).

  • The parse-intent layer: the +0.0276 F1 (vs the best well-documented

zero-shot system; +0.0226 vs the under-documented row) / lower-WER edge is on structured extraction, which is what an integration actually consumes — not raw transcription.

These are infrastructure and integration properties, not a benchmark number. The benchmark's honest job is to tell you the model gap is small; the product's job is the latency, cost, and parse-intent reliability around it. Conflating the two would be the dishonest move.
